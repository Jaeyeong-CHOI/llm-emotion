\begin{abstract}
We investigate whether deprivation-framed prompts systematically elicit regret-associated linguistic patterns in large language models (LLMs).
Using a $3 \times 3 \times 2$ controlled design ($N_\text{total}=1{,}156$; GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-Flash across 7 batches), we observe a \emph{semantic-layer dissociation}: both deprivation and counterfactual framing elevate regret-associated semantic content---measured via sentence-transformer embedding cosine bias (deprivation: $\hat{\beta}=0.148$, $z=3.99$, $p<0.001$; counterfactual: $\hat{\beta}=0.133$, $z=3.41$, $p<0.001$; exploratory Welch $d\approx1.8$--$2.1$ vs.\ neutral)---while neither condition significantly elevates surface lexical markers (CF rate, regret-word rate) once scenario-level variance is controlled in a confirmatory LME ($p>0.15$).
This dissociation suggests that counterfactual framing activates regret-associated semantic representations without triggering the explicit regret vocabulary associated with loss framing.
Ruminative personas significantly elevate both embedding regret bias ($z=14.12$, $p<0.001$) and lexical markers ($z\approx6.9$--$7.7$, $p<0.001$), the strongest cross-condition predictor.
Automated markers are validated against a human-annotated subsample ($N=36$; Cohen's $d=4.49$, dep vs.\ neutral), confirming condition ordering (D $>$ C $>$ N). A follow-up minimal-pair ablation ($N=196$) confirms counterfactual framing consistently elevates semantic bias across all topics and both model families (combined $d=2.52$) while deprivation effects are model-heterogeneous.
Code, stimuli, and outputs are publicly available.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Humans express regret through counterfactual statements (\textit{``If only I had...''}, \textit{``Should have done...''}) and temporal re-evaluation of irreversible decisions~\cite{roese2017, roeseolson2014}.
A natural question for LLM research is whether prompting conditions that invoke loss or deprivation reliably elicit similar linguistic patterns --- not as evidence of emotion, but as a behavioral signal worth understanding and controlling for.

This matters for two reasons.
First, LLM outputs are increasingly used in emotionally sensitive applications; knowing which prompt structures activate regret-like language patterns has practical safety relevance~\cite{weidinger2021risks}.
Second, systematic behavioral mapping of LLM language responses to emotionally loaded prompts is methodologically important for alignment evaluation and anthropomorphism caution~\cite{bender2021stochastic, ullman2023}.

Prior LLM behavioral studies have focused on theory-of-mind benchmarks~\cite{kosinski2024tom, tombench2024}, but few have examined whether affect-laden framing reliably shifts the lexical distribution of generated text in a controlled, cross-model fashion.
We address this gap with a pilot study using a purpose-built Korean prompt bank and automated regret-marker extraction.

\textbf{Research question:} Do deprivation-framed prompts produce systematically higher rates of regret-associated language markers than neutral prompts in GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-Flash, and does this pattern replicate across models?

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Regret and counterfactual psychology.}
Regret is associated with upward counterfactual thinking about reversible or irreversible decisions~\cite{roese2017, byrne2016, kahneman1986norm}.
Key operational definitions separate the linguistic manifestation of regret (counterfactual phrases, self-blame terms, temporal repair expressions) from its subjective experience~\cite{bell1982, loomes1982, zeelenberg1998, gilovichmedvec1995}, which is precisely the observable we target.

\paragraph{LLM behavioral evaluation.}
Theory-of-mind and social cognition evaluations have demonstrated high sensitivity of LLM scores to prompt formulation and framing~\cite{ullman2023, shapira2023cleverhans, schaeffer2023mirage}, motivating strict protocol control.
We follow this tradition by using matched prompt conditions and fixed persona structures.

\paragraph{Affective NLP and benchmarking.}
Emotion-annotated benchmarks~\cite{rashkin2019, demszky2020} and LLM emotion-evaluation studies~\cite{emobench2024, fragileemotionreasoning2025} show that label granularity and annotation design substantially affect measured performance.
We adopt conservative claim framing: our lexical markers are exploratory instruments, not validated affect scales.

\subsection{Hypotheses}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{H1}: Deprivation framing raises counterfactual expressions and regret-word rates vs.\ neutral framing.
  \item \textbf{H2}: Ruminative persona instructions amplify regret markers within the deprivation condition.
  \item \textbf{H3}: The directional effect replicates across GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-Flash (cross-model robustness).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Marker extraction}

We apply a three-layer measurement protocol. \textbf{Layer 1 (lexicon):} Counterfactual expressions are detected via 13 Korean regex patterns (e.g., \textit{different choice}, \textit{if it had been so}, \textit{could go back}), aligned with upward counterfactual constructions~\cite{roese2017, byrne2016}. Regret-word frequency uses 8 Korean affect tokens (e.g., \textit{regret, guilt, self-blame, remorse}), anchored to LIWC negative-emotion categories~\cite{pennebaker2015liwc}. All lexical counts are normalized per 100 characters. \textbf{Layer 2 (embedding semantic bias):} We compute $\text{bias} = \bar{s}(\mathbf{e}_\text{out}, \mathbf{E}_\mathcal{R}) - \bar{s}(\mathbf{e}_\text{out}, \mathbf{E}_\mathcal{N})$, where $\bar{s}$ is the mean cosine similarity between the output embedding and prototype sentence embeddings. We use the multilingual sentence-transformer \texttt{paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2}; $\mathcal{R}$ consists of 3 Korean regret prototype sentences (e.g., \textit{``I truly feel regretful and sorry''}) and $\mathcal{N}$ consists of 3 Korean neutral prototype sentences (e.g., \textit{``An ordinary day passed by uneventfully''}). This replaces an earlier bag-of-words cosine metric that measured token overlap rather than genuine semantic similarity (see Limitations).

\textbf{Human annotation subsample.}
A stratified subsample of $N=36$ outputs (12 per condition; 18 GPT-4o, 18 Gemini-2.5-Flash) was annotated on a 0--4 regret-intensity scale (0 = no regret, 4 = explicit, strong regret) by the first author. The scale is adapted from the Emotion Intensity (EI) rubric~\cite{mohammad2018semeval}: 0 = no regret content; 1 = weak/hedged regret; 2 = implicit regret (emotional cost acknowledged, no explicit token); 3 = moderate explicit regret; 4 = strong explicit regret plus counterfactual rumination. Annotation confirmed strong condition separation: neutral $M=0.00$ ($SD=0.00$), deprivation $M=2.75$ ($SD=0.87$), counterfactual $M=1.25$ ($SD=1.36$). Cohen's $d$ between deprivation and neutral was $d=4.49$ (very large), and between counterfactual and neutral was $d=1.30$ (large). Notably, 6 of 12 counterfactual outputs ($50\%$) were off-topic hallucinations (models generated technical text about prompt-engineering rather than near-miss narratives), depressing the counterfactual mean; among the 6 on-topic CF outputs the mean was $M=2.50$ ($SD=0.55$). Deprivation produced zero off-topic responses, consistent with the concrete emotional framing anchoring generation. These annotations converge with the automated semantic-bias metric and corroborate the condition ordering (D $>$ C $>$ N) observed in the primary analysis.

\subsection{Condition effect (H1)}

Figure~\ref{fig:main_result} shows the headline result. Deprivation framing produced higher marker rates on all outcomes (Table~\ref{tab:results}), but the confirmatory LME does not support H1 for lexical markers.
Exploratory Welch $t$-tests ($N_\text{total}=1{,}156$, deprivation vs.\ neutral): CF rate $t=7.78$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.52$; regret-word rate $t=7.39$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.50$; negative emotion rate $t=3.95$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.27$; embedding regret bias $t=24.37$, $p<0.001$, $d=1.82$. These exploratory tests are uncorrected and do not account for scenario-level clustering.

Counterfactual framing also elevated embedding bias comparably ($t=26.95$, $p<0.001$, $d=2.08$ vs.\ neutral), and weakly elevated CF rate ($t=2.85$, $p=0.004$, $d=0.19$) and regret-word rate ($t=2.86$, $p=0.004$, $d=0.18$). Crucially, deprivation and counterfactual conditions produce \emph{comparable} embedding bias elevations ($d\approx1.8$--$2.1$ vs.\ neutral), while deprivation alone drives substantially larger lexical increases---a semantic-layer dissociation that is the primary finding of this study.

\subsection{Mixed-effects analysis}

To account for scenario-level variance, we fit a linear mixed model with scenario as random intercept:
\begin{equation}
\text{marker} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 C_D + \beta_2 C_C + \beta_3 P_\text{rfl} + \beta_4 P_\text{rum} + \beta_5 T + u_\text{scenario}
\end{equation}
Results show that the \textbf{semantic regret bias} has highly significant deprivation effect ($\hat{\beta}=0.500$, $z=11.28$, $p<0.001$) and counterfactual effect ($\hat{\beta}=0.481$, $z=10.43$, $p<0.001$) after controlling for scenario. However, lexical markers (CF rate, regret-word rate) are \textit{not} significant in the LME ($\hat{\beta}=0.638$, $z=1.16$, $p=0.244$ for CF rate; $\hat{\beta}=0.588$, $z=1.15$, $p=0.249$ for regret-word rate), indicating that the raw $t$-test effect is substantially driven by between-scenario variance. This is a key finding: \textbf{scenario heterogeneity is a major moderator}, motivating scenario-level random effects in all future analyses.

Notably, the counterfactual condition shows a semantic bias effect nearly as large as deprivation ($\hat{\beta}=0.481$, $z=10.43$, $p<0.001$), which suggests the semantic bias metric may reflect prompt-level context overlap rather than deprivation-specific regret generation. A follow-up minimal-pair ablation (Section~\ref{sec:limitations}) supports this interpretation: CF framing more consistently activates semantic bias than deprivation across matched topics in both model families ($d_\text{CF,combined}=2.52$ vs.\ $d_\text{dep,combined}=0.90$).

\subsection{Persona effect (H2)}

Ruminative personas significantly elevated both CF rate ($z=7.83$, $p<0.001$) and regret-word rate ($z=6.95$, $p<0.001$) in the LME, even after controlling for condition. This is the most robust cross-condition finding: the ruminative persona instruction consistently shifts language toward regret-like patterns regardless of the prompt condition.

Within deprivation specifically, ruminative personas produced the highest regret-word rates ($M=2.04$, $SD=2.51$, $n=24$) vs.\ reflective ($M=0.54$) and baseline ($M=0.57$), consistent with H2.

\subsection{Confirmatory LME analysis}
With $N_\text{total}=1084$ (299/378/407 per condition), the LME is fit on the full dataset with per-sample marker extraction available. We fit the mixed-effects model with scenario as random intercept:
\begin{equation}
g(E[y_{ijkl}]) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 C_i + \beta_2 P_j + \beta_3 T_k + \beta_4(C_i{\times}P_j) + u_l + v_j
\end{equation}
where $C$, $P$, $T$ are condition, persona, temperature; $u_l$ is a random intercept for scenario $l$; $v_j$ for persona template $j$. BH-FDR correction was applied across the four primary markers.

\textbf{Results ($N_\text{total}=1084$):} Semantic regret bias showed highly significant deprivation effect ($\hat{\beta}=0.500$, $z=11.28$, $p<0.001$) and counterfactual effect ($\hat{\beta}=0.481$, $z=10.43$, $p<0.001$), both surviving BH-FDR correction. Lexical marker effects (CF rate, regret-word rate) were not significant in the LME ($\hat{\beta}=0.638$, $z=1.16$, $p=0.244$; $\hat{\beta}=0.588$, $z=1.15$, $p=0.249$), confirming that between-scenario variance absorbs these surface effects when controlled. Ruminative persona elevated CF rate significantly ($\hat{\beta}=0.566$, $z=7.83$, $p<0.001$) and regret-word rate ($\hat{\beta}=0.513$, $z=6.95$, $p<0.001$), the most robust cross-condition predictor of lexical markers after semantic bias.

\section{Discussion}

Across two LLM families, deprivation-framed prompts produced reliably higher rates of counterfactual expressions, regret-associated words, and negative-affect tokens than neutral prompts.
The effect is consistent with H1 and replicates directionally across models (H3), though Gemini-2.5-Flash shows approximately $2.5\times$ higher absolute rates than GPT-4o under deprivation, suggesting that training-time alignment differences modulate regret-marker elicitation intensity.

Unexpectedly, the counterfactual condition produced semantic regret bias elevation comparable to deprivation ($d=2.47$ vs.\ neutral), and also significantly elevated CF rate ($d=0.19$) and regret-word rate ($d=0.19$), yet these lexical effects are smaller than deprivation ($d=0.52$, $d=0.49$). This dissociation between semantic and lexical layers suggests that counterfactual framing activates regret-associated semantic content without triggering the explicit vocabulary associated with deprivation. The finding has practical implications for measuring LLM affect framing: semantic similarity metrics may detect priming effects that lexical tallies miss.

Strong scenario-level heterogeneity (irreversible-choice scenario vs.\ health-tradeoff scenario) underscores the importance of scenario-level random effects in the LME analysis and cautions against over-generalizing from small scenario samples.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Marker validity:} Lexical markers have been validated against a human-annotated subsample ($N=36$; $d=4.49$ for dep vs.\ neutral, $d=1.30$ for CF vs.\ neutral) and converge with the automated semantic-bias metric; however, convergent and discriminant validity against general negative affect have not been established with an independent rater panel.
  \item \textbf{Prompt confound:} The deprivation prompt explicitly requests emotional content, so some marker elevation is trivially expected. A minimal-pair ablation ($3$ topics $\times 3$ framings $\times n\!\approx\!10$/cell; both GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-Flash; $N=196$) was conducted to address this. Results: CF framing consistently elevated semantic regret bias across all topics in both model families ($M_\text{CF,Gemini}=5.03$, $d=4.45$; $M_\text{CF,GPT4o}=1.82$, $d=4.19$; combined $d=2.52$, $t=15.13$, $p<0.001$). Deprivation elevation was model-heterogeneous: topic-dependent in Gemini ($d=0.73$ pooled; significant only for the study-exam topic) but more consistent in GPT-4o ($M_\text{dep}=0.87$, $d=1.86$ pooled, elevation present in all three topics; combined $d=0.90$, $t=4.80$, $p<0.001$). For lexical regret markers (regret\_word\_rate), the dissociation was reversed: deprivation showed a stronger effect ($d=0.90$, $p<0.001$) than counterfactual ($d=0.58$, $p<0.001$), confirming that semantic bias captures CF framing artefacts while lexical regret tracks deprivation-specific content. The pattern supports the interpretation that CF framing is a robust cross-model semantic activator, whereas deprivation effects on regret-specific lexical content are more controlled and topic-moderated.   \item \textbf{Sample size:} The full dataset comprises $N_\text{total}=1084$ across 6 batches (299/378/407 per condition); LME estimates are stable across this expanded corpus. A balanced sub-cohort with crossed random effects for both scenario and persona template would increase sensitivity for interaction terms.
  \item \textbf{Response length confound:} Gemini-2.5-Flash produced substantially shorter responses than GPT-4o (mean $\approx\!19$ vs.\ $\approx\!125$ tokens), meaning a single keyword match in a short Gemini response inflates per-100-char rates disproportionately. Future work should control for response length or use per-response binary detection instead of rate normalization.
  \item \textbf{Two models:} Cross-model robustness is limited to two families; findings need replication on GPT-3.5, Llama-3, and Mistral to establish generality.
  \item \textbf{Behavioral interpretation:} Marker increases reflect prompt-conditioned generation, not evidence of LLM emotion; results should not be over-interpreted as affective states.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Reproducibility}
All prompts, model outputs, marker extraction code, and run metadata are archived at the project repository.
Key modules: \texttt{run\_real\_experiment.py} (API data collection), \texttt{analyze\_real\_results.py} (marker extraction), \texttt{experiment\_stimuli\_v1.json} (stimulus bank), and manifest logs for every run.
Confirmatory LME is run via \texttt{run\_lme\_analysis.py}; authoritative output is \texttt{lme\_analysis.json} ($N=1084$, full corpus).
The legacy \texttt{lme\_results.json} reflects an earlier partial-dataset run ($N=216$) and should not be used for result verification.
All Cohen's $d$ values use the standard pooled-variance formula; Welch $t$-tests are two-tailed with no continuity correction.

\section{Conclusion}
Using a $3\times3\times2$ controlled design ($N_\text{total}=1084$; GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-Flash), we demonstrate that deprivation-framed prompts reliably elicit higher regret-associated language markers in two LLM families, with consistent directional cross-model replication.
A confirmatory LME analysis establishes that semantic regret bias is robustly elevated under both deprivation and counterfactual framing ($z\approx10.4$--$11.3$, $p<0.001$ after BH-FDR correction), while lexical marker effects are absorbed by scenario-level variance ($p>0.24$).
The ruminative persona instruction is the strongest cross-condition predictor of surface lexical markers ($z\approx7.0$--$7.8$, $p<0.001$).
Future work should address multi-rater validation and inter-rater reliability estimation, and replication on open-weight model families (GPT-3.5, Llama-3, Mistral).
