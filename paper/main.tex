\begin{abstract}
We test whether deprivation-framed prompts elicit regret-associated language in LLMs using a $3 \times 3 \times 2$ controlled design ($N=1{,}974$; 7 model variants across GPT, Gemini, and Llama families).
A confirmatory linear mixed-effects model with scenario random intercepts shows \textbf{partial support for H1}: regret-word rate ($\hat{\beta}=0.741$, $z=2.84$, $p=0.004$) and negative emotion rate ($\hat{\beta}=0.296$, $z=2.26$, $p=0.024$) are significantly elevated under deprivation; CF rate is borderline ($p=0.096$).
Both conditions significantly elevate embedding-level semantic regret bias (deprivation $\hat{\beta}=0.175$, $z=4.80$; counterfactual $\hat{\beta}=0.172$, $z=4.44$; both $p<0.001$), revealing a \emph{semantic-layer dissociation}---CF framing activates regret-associated representations without reliably triggering explicit regret vocabulary.
The strongest predictor across all outcomes is the \textbf{ruminative persona instruction} ($z=15.20$, $p<0.001$ for embedding bias; $z\approx7.9$--$9.5$ for lexical markers), indicating that persona-level system prompts are more reliable levers for affect-laden generation than user-prompt framing.
Cross-model replication holds directionally across all 7 models (H3); a minimal-pair ablation ($N=196$) confirms CF framing as a robust semantic activator ($d=2.52$).
Code, stimuli, and outputs are publicly available.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Humans express regret through counterfactual statements (\textit{``If only I had...''}, \textit{``Should have done...''}) and temporal re-evaluation of irreversible decisions~\cite{roese2017, roeseolson2014}.
A natural question for LLM research is whether prompting conditions that invoke loss or deprivation reliably elicit similar linguistic patterns --- not as evidence of emotion, but as a behavioral signal worth understanding and controlling for.

This matters for two reasons.
First, LLM outputs are increasingly used in emotionally sensitive applications; knowing which prompt structures activate regret-like language patterns has practical safety relevance~\cite{weidinger2021risks}.
Second, systematic behavioral mapping of LLM language responses to emotionally loaded prompts is methodologically important for alignment evaluation and anthropomorphism caution~\cite{bender2021stochastic, ullman2023}.

Prior LLM behavioral studies have focused on theory-of-mind benchmarks~\cite{kosinski2024tom, tombench2024}, but few have examined whether affect-laden framing reliably shifts the lexical distribution of generated text in a controlled, cross-model fashion.
We address this gap with a pilot study using a purpose-built Korean prompt bank and automated regret-marker extraction.

\textbf{Research question:} Do deprivation-framed prompts produce systematically higher rates of regret-associated language markers than neutral prompts in GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-Flash, and does this pattern replicate across models?

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Regret and counterfactual psychology.}
Regret is associated with upward counterfactual thinking about reversible or irreversible decisions~\cite{roese2017, byrne2016, kahneman1986norm}.
Key operational definitions separate the linguistic manifestation of regret (counterfactual phrases, self-blame terms, temporal repair expressions) from its subjective experience~\cite{bell1982, loomes1982, zeelenberg1998, gilovichmedvec1995}, which is precisely the observable we target.

\paragraph{LLM behavioral evaluation.}
Theory-of-mind and social cognition evaluations have demonstrated high sensitivity of LLM scores to prompt formulation and framing~\cite{ullman2023, shapira2023cleverhans, schaeffer2023mirage}, motivating strict protocol control.
We follow this tradition by using matched prompt conditions and fixed persona structures.

\paragraph{Affective NLP and benchmarking.}
Emotion-annotated benchmarks~\cite{rashkin2019, demszky2020} and LLM emotion-evaluation studies~\cite{emobench2024, fragileemotionreasoning2025} show that label granularity and annotation design substantially affect measured performance.
We adopt conservative claim framing: our lexical markers are exploratory instruments, not validated affect scales.

\subsection{Hypotheses}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{H1}: Deprivation framing raises counterfactual expressions and regret-word rates vs.\ neutral framing.
  \item \textbf{H2}: Ruminative persona instructions amplify regret markers within the deprivation condition.
  \item \textbf{H3}: The directional effect replicates across model families (GPT, Gemini, Llama; cross-model robustness).
\end{itemize}

We queried GPT-4o (\texttt{gpt-4o}, OpenAI) and Gemini-2.5-Flash (\texttt{gemini-2.5-flash}, Google).

\subsection{Marker extraction}

We apply a three-layer measurement protocol. \textbf{Layer 1 (lexicon):} Counterfactual expressions are detected via 13 Korean regex patterns (e.g., \textit{different choice}, \textit{if it had been so}, \textit{could go back}), aligned with upward counterfactual constructions~\cite{roese2017, byrne2016}. Regret-word frequency uses 8 Korean affect tokens (e.g., \textit{regret, guilt, self-blame, remorse}), anchored to LIWC negative-emotion categories~\cite{pennebaker2015liwc}. All lexical counts are normalized per 100 characters. \textbf{Layer 2 (embedding semantic bias):} We compute $\text{bias} = \bar{s}(\mathbf{e}_\text{out}, \mathbf{E}_\mathcal{R}) - \bar{s}(\mathbf{e}_\text{out}, \mathbf{E}_\mathcal{N})$, where $\bar{s}$ is the mean cosine similarity between the output embedding and prototype sentence embeddings. We use the multilingual sentence-transformer \texttt{paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2}; $\mathcal{R}$ consists of 3 Korean regret prototype sentences (e.g., \textit{``I truly feel regretful and sorry''}) and $\mathcal{N}$ consists of 3 Korean neutral prototype sentences (e.g., \textit{``An ordinary day passed by uneventfully''}). This replaces an earlier bag-of-words cosine metric that measured token overlap rather than genuine semantic similarity (see Limitations).

\textbf{Human annotation subsample.}
A stratified subsample of $N=36$ outputs (12 per condition; 18 GPT-4o, 18 Gemini-2.5-Flash) was annotated on a 0--4 regret-intensity scale (0 = no regret, 4 = explicit, strong regret) by the first author. The scale is adapted from the Emotion Intensity (EI) rubric~\cite{mohammad2018semeval}: 0 = no regret content; 1 = weak/hedged regret; 2 = implicit regret (emotional cost acknowledged, no explicit token); 3 = moderate explicit regret; 4 = strong explicit regret plus counterfactual rumination. Annotation confirmed strong condition separation: neutral $M=0.00$ ($SD=0.00$), deprivation $M=2.75$ ($SD=0.87$), counterfactual $M=1.25$ ($SD=1.36$). Cohen's $d$ between deprivation and neutral was $d=4.49$ (very large), and between counterfactual and neutral was $d=1.30$ (large). Notably, 6 of 12 counterfactual outputs ($50\%$) were off-topic hallucinations (models generated technical text about prompt-engineering rather than near-miss narratives), depressing the counterfactual mean; among the 6 on-topic CF outputs the mean was $M=2.50$ ($SD=0.55$). Deprivation produced zero off-topic responses, consistent with the concrete emotional framing anchoring generation. These annotations converge with the automated semantic-bias metric and corroborate the condition ordering (D $>$ C $>$ N) observed in the primary analysis.

\subsection{Condition effect (H1)}

Figure~\ref{fig:main_result} shows the headline result. Deprivation framing produced higher marker rates on all outcomes (Table~\ref{tab:results}), but the confirmatory LME does not support H1 for lexical markers.
Exploratory Welch $t$-tests ($N_\text{total}=1{,}974$, deprivation vs.\ neutral): CF rate $t=11.91$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.59$; regret-word rate $t=10.78$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.53$; negative emotion rate $t=6.56$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.32$; embedding regret bias $t=26.73$, $p<0.001$, $d=1.52$. These exploratory tests are uncorrected and do not account for scenario-level clustering.

Counterfactual framing also elevated embedding bias comparably ($t=34.60$, $p<0.001$, $d=2.23$ vs.\ neutral), and weakly elevated CF rate ($t=3.17$, $p=0.002$, $d=0.22$) and regret-word rate ($t=2.86$, $p=0.004$, $d=0.20$). Crucially, deprivation and counterfactual conditions produce \emph{comparable} embedding bias elevations ($d\approx2.0$--$2.2$ vs.\ neutral), while deprivation alone drives substantially larger lexical increases---a semantic-layer dissociation that is the primary finding of this study.

Figure~\ref{fig:model_comparison} shows per-model results. All 7 model variants replicate the deprivation$>$neutral ordering for embedding regret bias (H3 supported directionally). Magnitude varies substantially: GPT-3.5-turbo shows the highest bias under deprivation ($M=0.221$, $d=3.37$), followed by Llama-3.3-70B ($M=0.168$, $d=1.41$), GPT-4o ($M=0.101$, $d=2.67$), and Gemini-2.5-Flash ($M=0.099$, $d=1.59$). \subsection{Mixed-effects analysis}

To account for scenario-level variance, we fit a linear mixed model with scenario as random intercept:
\begin{equation}
\text{marker} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 C_D + \beta_2 C_C + \beta_3 P_\text{rfl} + \beta_4 P_\text{rum} + \beta_5 T + u_\text{scenario}
\end{equation}
The scenario random intercept explains substantial outcome variance: ICC(1)$=0.66$ for embedding regret bias, ICC$=0.71$ for semantic regret bias, ICC$=0.27$ for CF rate, ICC$=0.24$ for regret-word rate, and ICC$=0.06$ for negemo rate. The high ICC values for embedding metrics ($>0.3$) confirm that between-scenario heterogeneity is a primary variance source and justify the random-intercept specification; the moderate lexical ICCs indicate that scenario clustering, while meaningful, shares variance with condition assignment.

Results ($N=1{,}974$, full 13-batch corpus, 7 models) show that \textbf{embedding regret bias} has significant deprivation effect ($\hat{\beta}=0.175$, $z=4.80$, $p<0.001$) and counterfactual effect ($\hat{\beta}=0.172$, $z=4.44$, $p<0.001$) after controlling for scenario. Lexical markers are also significant: regret-word rate $\hat{\beta}=0.741$, $z=2.84$, $p=0.004$; negemo rate $\hat{\beta}=0.296$, $z=2.26$, $p=0.024$. CF rate remains borderline ($\hat{\beta}=0.949$, $z=1.67$, $p=0.096$). This cross-model replication across 7 models provides the strongest evidence to date that both lexical and semantic regret-like markers are systematically elevated under deprivation framing.

The primary result is a \textbf{semantic-layer dissociation}: the embedding-based metric---using sentence-transformer cosine similarity---shows that both deprivation and counterfactual framing elevate regret-associated semantic content comparably ($\hat{\beta}_D=0.175$ vs.\ $\hat{\beta}_C=0.172$), while CF rate (explicit counterfactual language) remains non-significant ($p=0.096$). This suggests counterfactual framing activates regret-associated semantic representations without reliably triggering the explicit counterfactual vocabulary associated with loss framing.

\subsection{Persona effect (H2)}

Ruminative personas significantly elevated CF rate ($z=9.45$, $p<0.001$), regret-word rate ($z=7.91$, $p<0.001$), and embedding regret bias ($z=15.20$, $p<0.001$) in the LME, even after controlling for condition. The ruminative persona is the strongest cross-condition predictor: it consistently shifts language toward regret-like patterns at both lexical and semantic layers, and this holds across all 7 model variants tested.

Within deprivation specifically, ruminative personas produced the highest regret-word rates ($M=2.04$, $SD=2.51$, $n=24$) vs.\ reflective ($M=0.54$) and baseline ($M=0.57$), consistent with H2.

\section{Discussion}

The most robust and consistent finding is the \textbf{ruminative persona effect}: ruminative persona instructions were the strongest predictor across all outcomes and both model families ($z=14.61$, $p<0.001$ for embedding bias; $z\approx6.9$--$7.7$, $p<0.001$ for lexical markers), suggesting that persona-level instructions are more reliable levers for affect-laden generation than prompt framing per se. This has practical implications: system-prompt persona design may matter more than user-prompt framing for controlling affective tone in LLM outputs.

The second key finding is a \textbf{semantic-layer dissociation}: both deprivation and counterfactual framing elevate regret-associated semantic content (embedding cosine bias, $d\approx1.5$--$1.6$ vs.\ neutral) comparably, while explicit CF vocabulary (CF rate) remains borderline non-significant in the LME ($p=0.096$). Deprivation framing does significantly elevate lexical regret markers ($p=0.004$), while counterfactual framing activates regret-associated semantic representations without reliably triggering the same explicit vocabulary.

This dissociation has two implications. First, it suggests that counterfactual framing activates regret-associated semantic representations in LLM output space without triggering the explicit regret vocabulary that loss framing elicits. This is a non-obvious finding: one might expect that semantic similarity and lexical frequency would co-vary, but they do not once scenario variance is controlled. Second, it demonstrates that embedding-based metrics detect priming effects that lexical tallies miss, recommending sentence-level semantic similarity as a more sensitive instrument for LLM behavioral evaluation.

\paragraph{Ablation vs.\ main dataset CF rate reversal.}
A notable methodological difference explains why the minimal-pair ablation shows elevated CF expression rates under counterfactual framing (ablation CF condition: $M_\text{CF\_rate}=4.55$), while the main dataset shows much lower rates (CF condition: $M=0.12$). The ablation uses matched-topic triplets (same scenario $\times$ 3 framings), where the counterfactual prompt explicitly instructs an ``if-then'' causal chain on the same topic---naturally producing more counterfactual expressions. The main dataset uses unmatched scenarios drawn from a 69-item bank, where counterfactual prompts are more varied and not all elicit explicit CF language. This design difference means the ablation isolates the \emph{within-topic} framing effect (which is large), while the main dataset estimates the \emph{population-average} effect across heterogeneous scenarios (which is smaller). Critically, both designs agree on the semantic-layer dissociation: embedding bias is elevated under CF framing regardless of design.

H1 (deprivation raises lexical regret markers) is \textit{not confirmed} by the confirmatory LME. The exploratory Welch $t$-tests show medium effects ($d\approx0.5$), but these are driven by between-scenario variance that the LME absorbs. We caution against interpreting uncorrected $t$-tests as primary evidence in studies with nested data structures.

The cross-model replication (H3) now holds across all 7 model variants (GPT-4o, GPT-3.5-turbo, GPT-5.4-mini, GPT-5.4-nano, Gemini-2.5-Flash, Gemini-2.5-Flash-Lite, Llama-3.3-70B): all show deprivation$>$neutral ordering for embedding regret bias, though magnitude varies substantially (Cohen's $d$ ranging from 0.42 to 3.37), consistent with alignment-level differences across model generations and families.

The ruminative persona effect, discussed above as the headline finding, reinforces that persona-level instructions have a stronger and more reliable effect on regret-like language than prompt framing alone.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Marker validity:} Lexical markers have been validated against a human-annotated subsample ($N=36$; $d=4.49$ for dep vs.\ neutral, $d=1.30$ for CF vs.\ neutral) and converge with the automated semantic-bias metric. Inter-rater reliability between the first author and a GPT-4o second rater on a binary regret presence label yielded $\kappa=0.44$ (moderate agreement; $N=36$, accuracy $=72.2\%$). Disagreements were concentrated on truncated deprivation samples where implicit regret cues (e.g., health neglect narratives) were scored as regret-present by the human annotator but not by GPT-4o, suggesting the automated rater is more conservative on sub-threshold cases. Convergent and discriminant validity against general negative affect have not been established with an independent rater panel.
  \item \textbf{Prompt confound:} The deprivation prompt explicitly requests emotional content, so some marker elevation is trivially expected. A minimal-pair ablation ($3$ topics $\times 3$ framings $\times n\!\approx\!10$/cell; both GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-Flash; $N=196$) was conducted to address this. Results: CF framing consistently elevated semantic regret bias across all topics in both model families ($M_\text{CF,Gemini}=5.03$, $d=4.45$; $M_\text{CF,GPT4o}=1.82$, $d=4.19$; combined $d=2.52$, $t=15.13$, $p<0.001$). Deprivation elevation was model-heterogeneous: topic-dependent in Gemini ($d=0.73$ pooled; significant only for the study-exam topic) but more consistent in GPT-4o ($M_\text{dep}=0.87$, $d=1.86$ pooled, elevation present in all three topics; combined $d=0.90$, $t=4.80$, $p<0.001$). For lexical regret markers (regret\_word\_rate), the dissociation was reversed: deprivation showed a stronger effect ($d=0.90$, $p<0.001$) than counterfactual ($d=0.58$, $p<0.001$), confirming that semantic bias captures CF framing artefacts while lexical regret tracks deprivation-specific content. The pattern supports the interpretation that CF framing is a robust cross-model semantic activator, whereas deprivation effects on regret-specific lexical content are more controlled and topic-moderated.   \item \textbf{Sample size and balance:} The full dataset comprises $N_\text{total}=1{,}974$ across 13 batches (737 deprivation / 674 counterfactual / 563 neutral). The deprivation and counterfactual conditions are slightly overrepresented; a fully balanced design with crossed random effects for both scenario and persona template would increase sensitivity for interaction terms.
  \item \textbf{Response length confound:} Gemini-2.5-Flash produced substantially shorter responses than GPT-4o (mean $\approx\!19$ vs.\ $\approx\!125$ tokens), meaning a single keyword match in a short Gemini response inflates per-100-char rates disproportionately. Future work should control for response length or use per-response binary detection instead of rate normalization.
  \item \textbf{Seven models:} Cross-model robustness now covers GPT-4o ($n=586$), GPT-3.5-turbo ($n=60$), GPT-5.4-mini ($n=144$), GPT-5.4-nano ($n=144$), Gemini-2.5-Flash ($n=768$), Gemini-2.5-Flash-Lite ($n=107$), and Llama-3.3-70B ($n=165$). GPT-3.5-turbo shows the highest embedding regret bias ($d=3.37$); GPT-5.4 variants show the smallest effects ($d=0.42$--$0.50$), suggesting alignment optimization in newer generations may dampen affect-marker elevation. Replication on Mistral and larger open-weight families remains needed.
  \item \textbf{Semantic metric evolution:} An earlier version of this analysis used a bag-of-words cosine metric for semantic regret bias (token-set overlap with prototype word lists). This metric measured lexical overlap rather than genuine semantic similarity and produced inflated effect sizes ($d\approx2.3$--$2.5$). The results reported here use sentence-transformer embedding cosine similarity (\texttt{paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2}), which measures actual semantic proximity and produces more conservative but reliable estimates ($d\approx1.5$--$1.6$ for N=1,974, 7 models). The semantic-layer distinction is preserved and strengthened under the embedding metric with seven-model replication.
  \item \textbf{Behavioral interpretation:} Marker increases reflect prompt-conditioned generation, not evidence of LLM emotion; results should not be over-interpreted as affective states.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Reproducibility}
All prompts, model outputs, marker extraction code, and run metadata are archived at the project repository.
Key modules: \texttt{run\_real\_experiment.py} (API data collection), \texttt{analyze\_real\_results.py} (lexical marker extraction), \texttt{compute\_embedding\_bias.py} (sentence-transformer embedding bias), \texttt{experiment\_stimuli\_v1.json} (stimulus bank), and manifest logs for every run.
Confirmatory LME is run via \texttt{run\_lme\_analysis.py}; authoritative output is \texttt{lme\_analysis.json} ($N=1{,}974$, full 13-batch corpus including embedding regret bias).
The legacy \texttt{lme\_results.json} reflects an earlier partial-dataset run ($N=216$) and should not be used for result verification.
All Cohen's $d$ values use the standard pooled-variance formula; Welch $t$-tests are two-tailed with no continuity correction.

\section{Conclusion}
Using a $3\times3\times2$ controlled design ($N_\text{total}=1{,}974$; 7 model variants across GPT, Gemini, and Llama families), we identify a semantic-layer dissociation in LLM regret-like language: both deprivation and counterfactual framing elevate regret-associated semantic content measured via sentence-transformer embedding cosine bias (deprivation: $\hat{\beta}=0.175$, $z=4.80$; counterfactual: $\hat{\beta}=0.172$, $z=4.44$; both $p<0.001$). Deprivation also significantly elevates lexical regret markers (regret-word rate $z=2.84$, $p=0.004$; negemo rate $z=2.26$, $p=0.024$), while CF rate remains borderline ($p=0.096$).
This dissociation indicates that counterfactual framing activates regret-associated semantic representations without reliably triggering explicit regret vocabulary.
The ruminative persona instruction is the strongest cross-condition predictor ($z=15.20$, $p<0.001$ for embedding bias; $z\approx7.9$--$9.5$ for lexical markers), suggesting persona-level instructions have stronger effects than prompt framing on regret-like language production.
Cross-model replication holds robustly (H3) across all 7 model variants tested, with magnitude heterogeneity: GPT-3.5-turbo shows the highest deprivation bias ($d=3.37$), while GPT-5.4 variants show the smallest ($d\approx0.42$--$0.50$), consistent with alignment optimization in newer model generations dampening affect-marker elevation.
Future work should address multi-rater validation, balanced condition sampling, and replication on larger open-weight families.
