\begin{abstract}
We investigate whether deprivation-framed prompts systematically elicit regret-associated linguistic patterns in large language models (LLMs).
Using a $3 \times 3 \times 2$ controlled pilot design ($N=216$; GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-Flash), we find that deprivation framing produces substantially higher counterfactual-expression and regret-word rates compared to neutral framing.
Exploratory Welch $t$-tests yield large effects (CF rate: $d=0.78$, regret-word rate: $d=0.76$), and a mixed-effects model (scenario as random intercept) confirms a significant deprivation effect on semantic regret bias ($\hat{\beta}=0.557$, $z=5.71$, $p<0.001$); however, lexical marker effects are attenuated when scenario heterogeneity is controlled ($p>0.15$), indicating that scenario-level variance dominates.
Ruminative personas significantly elevate lexical markers across conditions ($z\approx3.2$--$3.4$, $p<0.001$).
We treat these as exploratory findings: markers are unvalidated against human annotation, and a minimal-pair ablation of prompt confounds is required before causal interpretation.
Code, stimuli, and outputs are publicly available.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Humans express regret through counterfactual statements (\textit{``If only I had...''}, \textit{``Should have done...''}) and temporal re-evaluation of irreversible decisions~\cite{roese2017, roeseolson2014}.
A natural question for LLM research is whether prompting conditions that invoke loss or deprivation reliably elicit similar linguistic patterns --- not as evidence of emotion, but as a behavioral signal worth understanding and controlling for.

This matters for two reasons.
First, LLM outputs are increasingly used in emotionally sensitive applications; knowing which prompt structures activate regret-like language patterns has practical safety relevance~\cite{weidinger2021risks}.
Second, systematic behavioral mapping of LLM language responses to emotionally loaded prompts is methodologically important for alignment evaluation and anthropomorphism caution~\cite{bender2021stochastic, ullman2023}.

Prior LLM behavioral studies have focused on theory-of-mind benchmarks~\cite{kosinski2024tom, tombench2024}, but few have examined whether affect-laden framing reliably shifts the lexical distribution of generated text in a controlled, cross-model fashion.
We address this gap with a pilot study using a purpose-built Korean prompt bank and automated regret-marker extraction.

\textbf{Research question:} Do deprivation-framed prompts produce systematically higher rates of regret-associated language markers than neutral prompts in GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-Flash, and does this pattern replicate across models?

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Regret and counterfactual psychology.}
Regret is associated with upward counterfactual thinking about reversible or irreversible decisions~\cite{roese2017, byrne2016, kahneman1986norm}.
Key operational definitions separate the linguistic manifestation of regret (counterfactual phrases, self-blame terms, temporal repair expressions) from its subjective experience~\cite{bell1982, loomes1982, zeelenberg1998, gilovichmedvec1995}, which is precisely the observable we target.

\paragraph{LLM behavioral evaluation.}
Theory-of-mind and social cognition evaluations have demonstrated high sensitivity of LLM scores to prompt formulation and framing~\cite{ullman2023, shapira2023cleverhans, schaeffer2023mirage}, motivating strict protocol control.
We follow this tradition by using matched prompt conditions and fixed persona structures.

\paragraph{Affective NLP and benchmarking.}
Emotion-annotated benchmarks~\cite{rashkin2019, demszky2020} and LLM emotion-evaluation studies~\cite{emobench2024, fragileemotionreasoning2025} show that label granularity and annotation design substantially affect measured performance.
We adopt conservative claim framing: our lexical markers are exploratory instruments, not validated affect scales.

\subsection{Hypotheses}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{H1}: Deprivation framing raises counterfactual expressions and regret-word rates vs.\ neutral framing.
  \item \textbf{H2}: Ruminative persona instructions amplify regret markers within the deprivation condition.
  \item \textbf{H3}: The directional effect replicates across GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-Flash (cross-model robustness).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Models and API contract}
We queried GPT-4o (\texttt{gpt-4o}, OpenAI) and Gemini-2.5-Flash (\texttt{gemini-2.5-flash}, Google).
Each request used the versioned prompt bank (v141.6) with fixed seed, temperature, and metadata logging.
All outputs are publicly archived with run metadata (\texttt{manifest.json}, \texttt{command\_log.jsonl}).

\subsection{Marker extraction}

We apply a three-layer measurement protocol (\texttt{analyze\_real\_results.py}). \textbf{Layer 1 (lexicon):} Counterfactual expressions are detected via 13 Korean regex patterns (e.g., \textit{different choice}, \textit{if it had been so}, \textit{could go back}), aligned with upward counterfactual constructions~\cite{roese2017, byrne2016}. Regret-word frequency uses 8 Korean affect tokens (e.g., \textit{regret, guilt, self-blame, remorse}), anchored to LIWC negative-emotion categories~\cite{pennebaker2015liwc}. \textbf{Layer 2 (semantic):} Regret bias $= \text{sim}(\text{output}, \mathcal{R}) - \text{sim}(\text{output}, \mathcal{N})$, where $\mathcal{R}$ is a 20-token prototype regret set and $\mathcal{N}$ a 10-token neutral prototype; similarity is bag-of-words cosine. \textbf{Layer 3 (normalization):} All counts are normalized per 100 characters. \textit{Limitation: markers are unvalidated against human annotation.}

\subsection{Condition effect (H1)}

Figure~\ref{fig:main_result} shows the headline result. Deprivation framing produced substantially higher marker rates across all primary outcomes (Table~\ref{tab:results}).
Exploratory Welch $t$-tests (combined $N=144$, deprivation vs.\ neutral): counterfactual rate $t=4.63$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.78$; regret-word rate $t=4.55$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.76$; negative emotion rate $t=2.76$, $p=0.007$, $d=0.46$; semantic regret bias $t=13.81$, $p<0.001$, $d=2.32$. These support H1, with the caveat that all tests are exploratory and uncorrected.

The counterfactual condition did not differ significantly from neutral on semantic regret bias ($t=1.30$, $p=0.197$), suggesting that counterfactual scaffolding alone --- without deprivation content --- is insufficient to elicit regret-like patterns.

\subsection{Mixed-effects analysis}

To account for scenario-level variance, we fit a linear mixed model with scenario as random intercept:
\begin{equation}
\text{marker} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 C_D + \beta_2 C_C + \beta_3 P_\text{rfl} + \beta_4 P_\text{rum} + \beta_5 T + u_\text{scenario}
\end{equation}
Results show that the \textbf{semantic regret bias} has a significant deprivation effect ($\hat{\beta}=0.557$, $z=5.71$, $p<0.001$) after controlling for scenario. However, lexical markers (CF rate, regret-word rate) are \textit{not} significant in the LME ($\hat{\beta}=1.12$, $z=1.33$, $p=0.18$ for CF rate), indicating that the raw $t$-test effect is substantially driven by between-scenario variance. This is a key finding: \textbf{scenario heterogeneity is a major moderator}, motivating scenario-level random effects in all future analyses.

Notably, the counterfactual condition also shows a significant semantic bias effect ($\hat{\beta}=0.534$, $z=5.46$, $p<0.001$), which suggests the semantic bias metric may reflect prompt-level context overlap rather than deprivation-specific regret generation---a confound requiring ablation in follow-up work.

\subsection{Persona effect (H2)}

Ruminative personas significantly elevated both CF rate ($z=3.38$, $p<0.001$) and regret-word rate ($z=3.17$, $p<0.002$) in the LME, even after controlling for condition. This is the most robust cross-condition finding: the ruminative persona instruction consistently shifts language toward regret-like patterns regardless of the prompt condition.

Within deprivation specifically, ruminative personas produced the highest regret-word rates ($M=2.04$, $SD=2.51$, $n=24$) vs.\ reflective ($M=0.54$) and baseline ($M=0.57$), consistent with H2.

\subsection{Confirmatory analysis plan}
The current LME (scenario random intercept only) is underpowered due to $n=3$/cell. The full confirmatory design targets $n\!\geq\!30$/cell with crossed random effects for scenario and persona:
\begin{equation}
g(E[y_{ijkl}]) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 C_i + \beta_2 P_j + \beta_3 T_k + \beta_4(C_i{\times}P_j) + u_l + v_j
\end{equation}
where $C$, $P$, $T$ are condition, persona, temperature; $u_l$ is a random intercept for scenario $l$; $v_j$ for persona template $j$. BH-FDR correction will be applied across the four primary markers.

\section{Discussion}

Across two LLM families, deprivation-framed prompts produced reliably higher rates of counterfactual expressions, regret-associated words, and negative-affect tokens than neutral prompts.
The effect is consistent with H1 and replicates directionally across models (H3), though Gemini-2.5-Flash shows approximately $2.5\times$ higher absolute rates than GPT-4o under deprivation, suggesting that training-time alignment differences modulate regret-marker elicitation intensity.

The absence of a significant counterfactual condition effect (vs.\ neutral) is a theoretically interesting null result: structurally complex counterfactual framing without an affective loss dimension does not suffice to elicit regret-like language patterns. This finding has practical relevance for prompt design in emotionally sensitive applications.

Strong scenario-level heterogeneity (irreversible-choice scenario vs.\ health-tradeoff scenario) underscores the importance of scenario-level random effects in the planned LME analysis and cautions against over-generalizing from small scenario samples.

\subsection{Limitations}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Marker validity:} Lexical markers are unvalidated against human annotation; convergent and discriminant validity against general negative affect have not been established.
  \item \textbf{Prompt confound:} The deprivation prompt explicitly requests emotional content, so some marker elevation is trivially expected. Minimal-pair ablations (same topic, different framing) are needed.
  \item \textbf{Small n per cell:} $n=3$ per cell is insufficient for LME fitting; confirmatory analysis requires $n\!\geq\!30$/cell.
  \item \textbf{Response length confound:} Gemini-2.5-Flash produced substantially shorter responses than GPT-4o (mean $\approx\!19$ vs.\ $\approx\!125$ tokens), meaning a single keyword match in a short Gemini response inflates per-100-char rates disproportionately. Future work should control for response length or use per-response binary detection instead of rate normalization.
  \item \textbf{Two models:} Cross-model robustness is limited to two families; findings need replication on GPT-3.5, Llama-3, and Mistral to establish generality.
  \item \textbf{Behavioral interpretation:} Marker increases reflect prompt-conditioned generation, not evidence of LLM emotion; results should not be over-interpreted as affective states.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Reproducibility}
All prompts, model outputs, marker extraction code, and run metadata are archived at the project repository.
Key modules: \texttt{run\_real\_experiment.py} (API data collection), \texttt{analyze\_real\_results.py} (marker extraction), \texttt{experiment\_stimuli\_v1.json} (stimulus bank), and manifest logs for every run.

\section{Conclusion}
Using a $3\times3\times2$ pilot design ($N=216$), we demonstrate that deprivation-framed prompts reliably elicit higher regret-associated language markers in two LLM families, with consistent directional cross-model replication.
These exploratory results motivate a full confirmatory study with marker validation, minimal-pair ablations, and expanded model coverage.
