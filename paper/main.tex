\begin{abstract}
This study investigates whether large language models (LLMs) exhibit language behavior analogous to human regret-like responses under loss and deprivation-like scenarios.
We do not claim that LLMs possess emotion; instead, we evaluate observable behavior under controlled prompting and reproducible infrastructure.

Unlike isolated prompt-completion experiments, our contribution is an integrated pipeline that combines literature discovery and quality triage, prompt-bank construction, standardized marker analysis, and continuous reporting for long-running experiments.

Current results remain preliminary: we report implementation outcomes from a synthetic stress-tested benchmark and prepare the system for real-model inference.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Keywords}
LLM behavior, regret-like language, counterfactual expression, reproducibility, automation, pipeline, literature screening

\section{Introduction}
Humans often express regret through counterfactual statements, self-attribution, and repeated reflection on irreversible decisions.
This motivates a testable question for LLM research:

\begin{quote}
\small Under comparable elicitation conditions, do LLM outputs show consistent markers associated with regret-like behavior?
\end{quote}

A key challenge is avoiding over-interpretation: apparent social behavior in LLMs can arise from prompt artifacts.
Recent findings in theory-of-mind and anthropomorphism show that performance is sensitive to task framing, so evaluation should be behaviorally grounded.

This work builds a transparent experimental stack where generation, logging, screening quality, and reporting are unified.

\section{Related Work}
We organize prior work into three streams.

\subsection{Human counterfactual and regret psychology}
Human regret research identifies markers such as:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Counterfactual language (\textit{if only ...}).
  \item Temporal revisiting of irreversible choices.
  \item Self-attribution and responsibility phrasing.
\end{itemize}
These provide the basis for marker definitions.

\subsection{LLM social-cognitive behavior}
Theory-of-mind benchmark work indicates that apparent perspective-taking behavior in LLMs is often fragile to prompt style.
This motivates fixed, explicit protocol design.

\subsection{Anthropomorphic attribution}
Human users often attribute emotion and intent to chatbots.
Thus behavioral evidence must be distinguished from affective-state claims.

\section{Research Design}
\subsection{Research questions}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Do deprivation/regret frames increase regret markers compared with neutral framing?
  \item Do persona instructions and temperature change marker strength and lexical variance?
  \item Can the full process remain reproducible under continuous automation?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Hypotheses}
\begin{itemize}
  \item H1: Deprivation framing raises counterfactual expressions.
  \item H2: Reflective personas increase self-attribution terms.
  \item H3: Higher decoding variance changes wording but does not erase prompt-condition effects.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Automated literature workflow}
Candidate retrieval and screening run in background jobs.
Quality gates track balance, entropy, confidence, source diversity, risk diversity, query coverage, and traceability.

\subsection{Prompt and condition design}
Current bank: 436 scenarios, 311 personas, 5 temperatures (0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.7, 1.0).
Generation conditions are the Cartesian product of scenario group, persona group, and temperature.

\subsection{Markers and analysis}
For each sample we compute:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Counterfactual marker frequency.
  \item Regret-word frequency.
  \item Condition-aggregated means/standard deviations.
  \item Basic entropy/coverage checks.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}
This study currently demonstrates a reproducible research stack.
The primary contribution is not final behavioral claims, but an auditable system that links literature screening, experimental design, and execution state.

\subsection{Limitations}
The current outputs are synthetic.
Hence interpretations are provisional and should not be used as final psychological conclusions.

\section{Conclusion and next step}
We provide a pipeline-oriented framework for regret-like language studies in LLMs.
Immediate next steps are to replace mock data with production model outputs, run stronger statistical tests, and finalize a gold-set-reviewed literature corpus.
