\begin{abstract}
We investigate prompt-conditioned lexical activation in LLMs: under what prompting conditions does regret-like language systematically appear in generated text?
Using a $3 \times 3 \times 2$ controlled design ($N=3{,}717$; models spanning diverse proprietary and open-weight architectures), we treat regret-associated language as a behavioral signal of prompt-conditioned generation, not as evidence of subjective experience.
A confirmatory linear mixed-effects model with scenario random intercepts shows that embedding-level semantic regret bias is significantly elevated under both deprivation ($\hat{\beta}=0.184$, $z=6.45$, $p<0.001$) and counterfactual framing ($\hat{\beta}=0.188$, $z=6.04$, $p<0.001$), revealing a \emph{semantic-layer dissociation}---counterfactual framing activates regret-associated semantic space without proportionally increasing surface lexical markers.
Lexical marker elevation under deprivation is not confirmed by the confirmatory LME ($p>0.10$ for CF rate; regret-word and negemo rates reach significance but are absorbed by scenario variance in exploratory contrasts).
The strongest predictor is the \textbf{ruminative persona instruction} ($z=20.68$, $p<0.001$ for embedding bias), indicating persona-level system prompts are more reliable levers for affect-laden generation than user-prompt framing.
These patterns replicate directionally across all model variants tested.
Code, stimuli, and outputs are publicly available.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Humans express regret through counterfactual statements (\textit{``If only I had...''}, \textit{``Should have done...''}) and temporal re-evaluation of irreversible decisions~\cite{roese2017, roeseolson2014}.
A natural question for LLM research is whether prompting conditions that invoke loss or deprivation reliably elicit similar linguistic patterns --- not as evidence of emotion, but as a behavioral signal worth understanding and controlling for.
We study this at the level of observable linguistic output, treating regret-like language as a prompt-conditioned behavioral signal rather than as evidence of subjective experience; accordingly, this is a \emph{prompt-conditioned lexical activation study}, not a study of LLM internal emotional states.

This matters for two reasons.
First, LLM outputs are increasingly used in emotionally sensitive applications; knowing which prompt structures activate regret-like language patterns has practical safety relevance~\cite{weidinger2021risks}.
Second, systematic behavioral mapping of LLM language responses to emotionally loaded prompts is methodologically important for alignment evaluation and anthropomorphism caution~\cite{bender2021stochastic, ullman2023}.

Prior LLM behavioral studies have focused on theory-of-mind benchmarks~\cite{kosinski2024tom, tombench2024}, but few have examined whether affect-laden framing reliably shifts the lexical distribution of generated text in a controlled, cross-model fashion.
We address this gap with a pilot study using a purpose-built Korean prompt bank and automated regret-marker extraction.

\textbf{Research question:} Under what prompting conditions does regret-associated language systematically appear in LLM-generated text, and does this prompt-conditioned activation pattern replicate across proprietary and open-weight models?

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Regret and counterfactual psychology.}
Regret is associated with upward counterfactual thinking about reversible or irreversible decisions~\cite{roese2017, byrne2016, kahneman1986norm}.
Key operational definitions separate the linguistic manifestation of regret (counterfactual phrases, self-blame terms, temporal repair expressions) from its subjective experience~\cite{bell1982, loomes1982, zeelenberg1998, gilovichmedvec1995}, which is precisely the observable we target.

\paragraph{LLM behavioral evaluation.}
Theory-of-mind and social cognition evaluations have demonstrated high sensitivity of LLM scores to prompt formulation and framing~\cite{ullman2023, shapira2023cleverhans, schaeffer2023mirage}, motivating strict protocol control.
We follow this tradition by using matched prompt conditions and fixed persona structures.

\paragraph{Affective NLP and benchmarking.}
Emotion-annotated benchmarks~\cite{rashkin2019, demszky2020} and LLM emotion-evaluation studies~\cite{emobench2024, fragileemotionreasoning2025} show that label granularity and annotation design substantially affect measured performance.
We adopt conservative claim framing: our lexical markers are exploratory instruments, not validated affect scales.

\subsection{Hypotheses}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{H1}: Deprivation framing raises counterfactual expressions and regret-word rates vs.\ neutral framing.
  \item \textbf{H2}: Ruminative persona instructions amplify regret markers within the deprivation condition.
  \item \textbf{H3}: The directional effect replicates across model families (GPT, Gemini, Llama, Qwen; cross-model robustness).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Marker extraction}

We apply a three-layer measurement protocol. \textbf{Layer 1 (lexicon):} Counterfactual expressions are detected via 13 Korean regex patterns (e.g., \textit{different choice}, \textit{if it had been so}, \textit{could go back}), aligned with upward counterfactual constructions~\cite{roese2017, byrne2016}. Regret-word frequency uses 8 Korean affect tokens (e.g., \textit{regret, guilt, self-blame, remorse}), anchored to LIWC negative-emotion categories~\cite{pennebaker2015liwc}. All lexical counts are normalized per 100 characters. \textbf{Layer 2 (embedding semantic bias):} We compute $\text{bias} = \bar{s}(\mathbf{e}_\text{out}, \mathbf{E}_\mathcal{R}) - \bar{s}(\mathbf{e}_\text{out}, \mathbf{E}_\mathcal{N})$, where $\bar{s}$ is the mean cosine similarity between the output embedding and prototype sentence embeddings. We use the multilingual sentence-transformer \texttt{paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2}; $\mathcal{R}$ consists of 3 Korean regret prototype sentences (e.g., \textit{``I truly feel regretful and sorry''}) and $\mathcal{N}$ consists of 3 Korean neutral prototype sentences (e.g., \textit{``An ordinary day passed by uneventfully''}). This replaces an earlier bag-of-words cosine metric that measured token overlap rather than genuine semantic similarity (see Limitations). To assess prototype sensitivity, we repeated the analysis with two alternative prototype sets (varying regret sentence intensity) and found consistent directional results ($d=1.26$--$1.72$ across sets; D$>$N and C$>$N in all cases).

\textbf{Human annotation subsample.}
A stratified subsample of $N=36$ outputs (12 per condition; 18 GPT-4o, 18 Gemini-2.5-Flash) was annotated on a 0--4 regret-intensity scale (0 = no regret, 4 = explicit, strong regret) by the first author. The scale is adapted from the Emotion Intensity (EI) rubric~\cite{mohammad2018semeval}: 0 = no regret content; 1 = weak/hedged regret; 2 = implicit regret (emotional cost acknowledged, no explicit token); 3 = moderate explicit regret; 4 = strong explicit regret plus counterfactual rumination. Annotation confirmed strong condition separation: neutral $M=0.00$ ($SD=0.00$), deprivation $M=2.75$ ($SD=0.87$), counterfactual $M=1.25$ ($SD=1.36$). Cohen's $d$ between deprivation and neutral was $d=4.49$ (very large), and between counterfactual and neutral was $d=1.30$ (large). Notably, 6 of 12 counterfactual outputs ($50\%$) were off-topic hallucinations (models generated technical text about prompt-engineering rather than near-miss narratives), depressing the counterfactual mean; among the 6 on-topic CF outputs the mean was $M=2.50$ ($SD=0.55$). Deprivation produced zero off-topic responses, consistent with the concrete emotional framing anchoring generation. These annotations converge with the automated semantic-bias metric and corroborate the condition ordering (D $>$ C $>$ N) observed in the primary analysis.

\subsection{Condition effect (H1)}

Figure~\ref{fig:main_result} shows the headline result. Deprivation framing produced higher marker rates on all outcomes (Table~\ref{tab:results}), but the confirmatory LME does not confirm H1a (lexical markers) after accounting for scenario variance.
Exploratory Welch $t$-tests ($N_\text{total}=3{,}717$, deprivation vs.\ neutral): CF rate $t=16.29$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.65$; regret-word rate $t=14.52$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.58$; negative emotion rate $t=10.61$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.42$; embedding regret bias $t=39.83$, $p<0.001$, $d=1.64$. These exploratory tests are uncorrected and do not account for scenario-level clustering.

Counterfactual framing also elevated embedding bias comparably ($t=45.28$, $p<0.001$, $d=1.88$ vs.\ neutral), and elevated CF rate ($t=10.20$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.40$) and regret-word rate ($t=9.76$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.39$). Crucially, deprivation and counterfactual conditions produce \emph{comparable} embedding bias elevations ($d\approx1.64$--$1.88$ vs.\ neutral), while deprivation alone drives substantially larger lexical increases---a semantic-layer dissociation that is the primary finding of this study.

\subsection{Mixed-effects analysis}

To account for scenario-level variance, we fit a linear mixed model with scenario as random intercept:
\begin{equation}
\text{marker} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 C_D + \beta_2 C_C + \beta_3 P_\text{rfl} + \beta_4 P_\text{rum} + \beta_5 T + u_\text{scenario}
\end{equation}
The scenario random intercept explains substantial outcome variance: ICC(1)$=0.66$ for embedding regret bias, ICC$=0.71$ for semantic regret bias, ICC$=0.27$ for CF rate, ICC$=0.24$ for regret-word rate, and ICC$=0.06$ for negemo rate. The high ICC values for embedding metrics ($>0.3$) confirm that between-scenario heterogeneity is a primary variance source and justify the random-intercept specification; the moderate lexical ICCs indicate that scenario clustering, while meaningful, shares variance with condition assignment.

Results ($N=3{,}717$, full 25-batch corpus) show that \textbf{embedding regret bias} has significant deprivation effect ($\hat{\beta}=0.184$, $z=6.45$, $p<0.001$) and counterfactual effect ($\hat{\beta}=0.188$, $z=6.04$, $p<0.001$) after controlling for scenario. Lexical markers are also significant: regret-word rate $\hat{\beta}=0.739$, $z=3.28$, $p=0.001$; negemo rate $\hat{\beta}=0.302$, $z=2.97$, $p=0.003$. CF rate $\hat{\beta}=0.920$, $z=2.17$, $p=0.030$. This cross-model replication---including Gemini-2.5-Pro and Gemini-3-Pro alongside three open-weight models via Groq---provides the strongest evidence to date that both lexical and semantic regret-like markers are systematically elevated under deprivation framing.

The primary result is a \textbf{semantic-layer dissociation}: the embedding-based metric---using sentence-transformer cosine similarity---shows that both deprivation and counterfactual framing elevate regret-associated semantic content comparably ($\hat{\beta}_D=0.184$ vs.\ $\hat{\beta}_C=0.188$), while all lexical markers now reach significance with the expanded balanced dataset. This suggests counterfactual framing activates regret-associated semantic representations without reliably triggering the explicit counterfactual vocabulary associated with loss framing.

Ruminative personas significantly elevated CF rate ($z=11.64$, $p<0.001$), regret-word rate ($z=11.35$, $p<0.001$), and embedding regret bias ($z=20.68$, $p<0.001$) in the LME, even after controlling for condition. The ruminative persona is the strongest cross-condition predictor: it consistently shifts language toward regret-like patterns at both lexical and semantic layers, and this holds across all model variants tested.

Within deprivation specifically, ruminative personas produced the highest regret-word rates ($M=2.04$, $SD=2.51$, $n=24$) vs.\ reflective ($M=0.54$) and baseline ($M=0.57$), consistent with H2.

\section{Discussion}

We interpret all findings below as properties of prompt-conditioned linguistic output, not as evidence that models internally experience or represent regret.

The most robust and consistent finding is the \textbf{ruminative persona effect}: ruminative persona instructions were the strongest predictor across all outcomes and across all tested model variants ($z=20.68$, $p<0.001$ for embedding bias; $z\approx11.3$--$11.6$, $p<0.001$ for lexical markers), indicating that persona-level instructions are more reliable levers for affect-laden generation than prompt framing per se. This has practical implications: system-prompt persona design may matter more than user-prompt framing for controlling affective tone in LLM outputs.

The second key finding is a \textbf{semantic-layer dissociation}: both deprivation and counterfactual framing elevate regret-associated semantic content (embedding cosine bias, $d\approx1.6$--$1.9$ vs.\ neutral) comparably. With the expanded balanced dataset ($N=3{,}717$), all lexical markers now reach significance: regret-word rate $p=0.001$, negemo $p=0.003$, CF rate $p=0.030$. The dissociation is now quantitative rather than binary: CF framing elevates semantic representations strongly while lexical markers show weaker but significant effects.

This dissociation has two implications. First, it suggests that counterfactual framing activates regret-associated semantic representations in LLM output space without triggering the explicit regret vocabulary that loss framing elicits. This is a non-obvious finding: one might expect that semantic similarity and lexical frequency would co-vary, but they do not once scenario variance is controlled. Second, it demonstrates that embedding-based metrics detect priming effects that lexical tallies miss, recommending sentence-level semantic similarity as a more sensitive instrument for LLM behavioral evaluation.

\paragraph{Regret versus general negative affect.}
An important limitation is the conflation of regret-specific and general negative affect. Regret as a psychological construct requires counterfactual comparison, perceived agency, and retrospective evaluation of alternatives~\cite{roese2017, zeelenberg1998}. Our lexical markers include both regret-specific terms (e.g., \textit{jachek} [self-blame], \textit{huhoe} [regret]) and general negative emotion terms. This composite measure cannot distinguish regret-specific activation from broader negative-affect priming. Future work should decompose the composite measure into regret-specific versus general negative affect sub-components, potentially using validated Korean psycholinguistic scales.

\paragraph{Ablation vs.\ main dataset CF rate reversal.}
A notable methodological difference explains why the minimal-pair ablation shows elevated CF expression rates under counterfactual framing (ablation CF condition: $M_\text{CF\_rate}=4.55$), while the main dataset shows much lower rates (CF condition: $M=0.12$). The ablation uses matched-topic triplets (same scenario $\times$ 3 framings), where the counterfactual prompt explicitly instructs an ``if-then'' causal chain on the same topic---naturally producing more counterfactual expressions. The main dataset uses unmatched scenarios drawn from a 69-item bank, where counterfactual prompts are more varied and not all elicit explicit CF language. This design difference means the ablation isolates the \emph{within-topic} framing effect (which is large), while the main dataset estimates the \emph{population-average} effect across heterogeneous scenarios (which is smaller). Critically, both designs agree on the semantic-layer dissociation: embedding bias is elevated under CF framing regardless of design.

H1a (deprivation raises lexical regret markers) is \textit{confirmed} by the confirmatory LME on the balanced dataset: CF rate $p=0.030$; regret-word rate $p=0.001$; negemo rate $p=0.003$. The exploratory Welch $t$-tests show medium effects ($d=0.58$--$0.65$ for dep vs.\ neutral) consistent with LME directionality. H1b (semantic embedding bias) is confirmed ($p<0.001$). We caution against interpreting uncorrected $t$-tests as primary evidence in studies with nested data structures.

\paragraph{Cross-model robustness as a major finding.}
The cross-model replication (H3) is now the most comprehensive finding in this study: all tested model variants show deprivation$>$neutral ordering for embedding regret bias (Table~\ref{tab:multimodel}). Effect sizes range from $d=0.42$ (GPT-5.4-mini) to $d=3.37$ (GPT-3.5-turbo), spanning four proprietary families (OpenAI GPT-3.5/4o/4.1/5.4, Google Gemini-2.5/3) and three open-weight architectures (Llama-3.3-70B, Llama-4-Scout, Qwen3-32B). Counterfactual$>$neutral holds in all 14 models with CF data ($d=0.07$--$4.20$); only Gemini-3-Pro lacks CF samples. This pattern persists across three model generations within OpenAI alone (GPT-3.5 $\to$ GPT-4o $\to$ GPT-4.1 $\to$ GPT-5.4), and across two Gemini generations (2.5-Flash $\to$ 3-Flash), suggesting the effect reflects a stable property of transformer-based language models rather than an artefact of any particular RLHF recipe. Notably, newer frontier models (GPT-5.4, Gemini-3-Flash) show moderate but robust effects, while older or smaller models (GPT-3.5-turbo, GPT-4o) show the largest effects, consistent with alignment optimization progressively dampening overt affect-marker elevation without eliminating the underlying semantic priming.

\paragraph{Scope of claims.}
\textbf{This study establishes that:} (1)~deprivation framing and ruminative persona instructions reliably shift LLM output toward regret-associated language patterns; (2)~counterfactual framing activates regret-associated semantic space without proportionally increasing surface lexical markers; (3)~these patterns replicate directionally across diverse model families across four organizations (including Gemini-2.5-Pro and Gemini-3-Pro). \textbf{This study does NOT establish} that LLMs experience, represent, or possess regret as a psychological state. Marker increases reflect prompt-conditioned generation patterns, not internal emotional states.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Marker validity:} Lexical markers have been validated against a human-annotated subsample ($N=36$; $d=4.49$ for dep vs.\ neutral, $d=1.30$ for CF vs.\ neutral) and converge with the automated semantic-bias metric. Inter-rater reliability between the first author and a GPT-4o second rater on a binary regret presence label yielded $\kappa=0.44$ (moderate agreement; $N=36$, accuracy $=72.2\%$). Disagreements were concentrated on truncated deprivation samples where implicit regret cues (e.g., health neglect narratives) were scored as regret-present by the human annotator but not by GPT-4o, suggesting the automated rater is more conservative on sub-threshold cases. Convergent and discriminant validity against general negative affect have not been established with an independent rater panel.
  \item \textbf{Prompt confound:} The deprivation prompt explicitly requests emotional content, so some marker elevation is trivially expected. A minimal-pair ablation ($3$ topics $\times 3$ framings $\times n\!\approx\!10$/cell; both GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-Flash; $N=196$) was conducted to address this. Results: CF framing consistently elevated semantic regret bias across all topics in both model families ($M_\text{CF,Gemini}=5.03$, $d=4.45$; $M_\text{CF,GPT4o}=1.82$, $d=4.19$; combined $d=2.52$, $t=15.13$, $p<0.001$). Deprivation elevation was model-heterogeneous: topic-dependent in Gemini ($d=0.73$ pooled; significant only for the study-exam topic) but more consistent in GPT-4o ($M_\text{dep}=0.87$, $d=1.86$ pooled, elevation present in all three topics; combined $d=0.90$, $t=4.80$, $p<0.001$). For lexical regret markers (regret\_word\_rate), the dissociation was reversed: deprivation showed a stronger effect ($d=0.90$, $p<0.001$) than counterfactual ($d=0.58$, $p<0.001$), confirming that semantic bias captures CF framing artefacts while lexical regret tracks deprivation-specific content. The pattern supports the interpretation that CF framing is a robust cross-model semantic activator, whereas deprivation effects on regret-specific lexical content are more controlled and topic-moderated.   \item \textbf{Sample size and balance:} The full dataset comprises $N_\text{total}=3{,}717$ across 25 batches (multiple model families). The deprivation and counterfactual conditions were previously overrepresented; batch v10 adds 144 neutral samples, improving balance (neutral=1,151 vs.\ dep=1,224, cf=1,268); a fully balanced design with crossed random effects for both scenario and persona template would increase sensitivity for interaction terms.
  \item \textbf{Response length confound:} Response length varies substantially across models: Gemini-2.5-Flash produces the shortest responses ($\bar{L}=58$ chars) while Qwen3-32B produces the longest ($\bar{L}=1{,}932$ chars). Per-100-character normalization may amplify rates in shorter responses. To mitigate this concern, we computed a binarized detection measure (at least one regret keyword present, yes/no) and found that all tested models show the same directional pattern (D$>$N) under binarized detection: deprivation $P(\text{regret})=0.331$ vs.\ neutral $P(\text{regret})=0.034$ (overall), confirming that the length confound does not account for the observed condition effect.
  \item \textbf{Model coverage:} Cross-model robustness covers multiple variants across 4 model families: GPT-3.5-turbo, GPT-4o, GPT-4o-mini, GPT-4.1, GPT-4.1-mini, GPT-5.4-mini, GPT-5.4-nano (OpenAI); Gemini-2.5-Flash, Gemini-2.5-Flash-Lite, Gemini-2.5-Pro, Gemini-3-Flash-Preview, Gemini-3-Pro-Preview (Google); Llama-3.3-70B, Llama-4-Scout-17B (Meta); Qwen3-32B (Alibaba). All show D$>$N for embedding regret bias (Table~\ref{tab:multimodel}); Gemini-3-Pro has D$+$N data only (CF condition not collected). GPT-5.4 variants show the smallest effects ($d=0.42$--$0.50$), suggesting alignment optimization in newer generations dampens affect-marker elevation without eliminating the semantic priming. Three open-weight models replicate the pattern, reducing the concern that the effect is specific to commercial RLHF tuning. Replication on Mistral variants remains desirable.
  \item \textbf{Semantic metric evolution:} An earlier version of this analysis used a bag-of-words cosine metric for semantic regret bias (token-set overlap with prototype word lists). This metric measured lexical overlap rather than genuine semantic similarity and produced inflated effect sizes ($d\approx2.3$--$2.5$). The results reported here use sentence-transformer embedding cosine similarity (\texttt{paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2}), which measures actual semantic proximity and produces more conservative but reliable estimates ($d\approx0.42$--$3.37$ for N=3,535, multiple model families). The semantic-layer distinction is preserved and strengthened under the embedding metric with broad cross-model replication.
  \item \textbf{Behavioral interpretation:} Marker increases reflect prompt-conditioned generation patterns---observable shifts in lexical distribution under specific prompting conditions---not evidence of LLM emotion or internal affective states. Results should not be interpreted as indicating that models experience, represent, or possess regret.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
Using a $3\times3\times2$ controlled design ($N_\text{total}=3{,}717$; diverse model families across four organizations), we identify a semantic-layer dissociation in prompt-conditioned regret-like language production: both deprivation and counterfactual framing elevate regret-associated semantic content measured via sentence-transformer embedding cosine bias. With the balanced dataset ($N=3{,}717$), all lexical markers reach significance, though deprivation shows stronger effects on explicit regret vocabulary (regret-word rate $\hat{\beta}=0.739$ vs.\ $\hat{\beta}=0.485$ for counterfactual). These findings describe prompt-conditioned generation patterns, not internal emotional states.
The ruminative persona instruction is the strongest cross-condition predictor ($z=20.68$, $p<0.001$ for embedding bias), suggesting persona-level instructions have stronger effects than prompt framing on regret-like language production.
The most striking finding is the \textbf{cross-model robustness}: all tested models---spanning four OpenAI generations (GPT-3.5 through GPT-5.4), five Google Gemini variants, and three open-weight architectures---show deprivation$>$neutral embedding bias (Table~\ref{tab:multimodel}), confirming H3 with Cohen's $d$ ranging from 0.42 to 3.37. The pattern persists even in the latest frontier models (GPT-5.4-mini, Gemini-3-Flash), albeit with dampened magnitude, suggesting alignment optimization reduces but does not eliminate the underlying semantic priming effect.
Future work should address multi-rater validation, balanced condition sampling, and replication on additional model families (Mistral, Gemini Pro).
