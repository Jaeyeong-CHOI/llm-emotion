\begin{abstract}
This study investigates whether large language models (LLMs) exhibit language behavior analogous to human regret-like responses under loss and deprivation-like scenarios.
We do not claim that LLMs possess emotion; instead, we evaluate observable behavior under controlled prompting and reproducible infrastructure.

Unlike isolated prompt-completion experiments, our contribution is an integrated pipeline that combines literature discovery and quality triage, prompt-bank construction, standardized marker analysis, and continuous reporting for long-running experiments.

Current results remain preliminary: we report implementation outcomes from a synthetic stress-tested benchmark and prepare the system for real-model inference.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Keywords}
LLM behavior, regret-like language, counterfactual expression, reproducibility, automation, pipeline, literature screening

\section{Introduction}
Humans often express regret through counterfactual statements, self-attribution, and repeated reflection on irreversible decisions.
This motivates a testable question for LLM research:

\begin{quote}
\small Under comparable elicitation conditions, do LLM outputs show consistent markers associated with regret-like behavior?
\end{quote}

A key challenge is avoiding over-interpretation: apparent social behavior in LLMs can arise from prompt artifacts.
Recent findings in theory-of-mind and anthropomorphism show that performance is sensitive to task framing, so evaluation should be behaviorally grounded.

This work builds a transparent experimental stack where generation, logging, screening quality, and reporting are unified.

\section{Related Work}
We reviewed 279 papers recovered from OpenAlex and organized them into five evidence-driven streams: counterfactual--regret psychology, LLM social-cognitive reasoning, anthropomorphism studies, emotion/benchmark methodology, and reproducibility-oriented design.

\subsection{Counterfactual and regret psychology}
Classical work on regret and counterfactual reasoning identifies measurable linguistic signatures of missed alternatives, such as upward counterfactual phrases, temporal re-evaluation, and responsibility attributions.
Roese and Epstude's synthesis is foundational for marker-based operationalization in non-affective constructs~\cite{roese2017, roese2008}.
These definitions support our focus on language-level observables rather than emotional-state inference. We also draw motivation from cognitive and neuroscience accounts of counterfactual evaluation and emotion computation, where behavioral signals are often separated from subjective reports.

\subsection{LLM social-cognitive behavior and theory-of-mind}
Theory-of-mind studies on LLMs show a mixed landscape: strong scores in some benchmark conditions but high sensitivity to prompts, instruction style, and surface perturbations.
Large-scale evaluations and stress tests~\cite{kosinski2024tom, strachan2024, tombench2024, opentom2024, simpletom2024, hito2023, mindgames2023} indicate that reproducibility requires strict protocol control and explicit coverage constraints.
Work on robustness under minor alterations\cite{ullman2023} and perspective-taking guidance\cite{ma2024} suggests that protocol variance can materially alter observed social reasoning.

\subsection{Anthropomorphic attribution and emotional intelligence}
A separate but adjacent literature shows that users attribute personality, emotion, and agency to AI text systems more readily than intended from engineering perspective.
User studies on anthropomorphic chat interfaces~\cite{i_chatbot_2023, anthro2023} and affective-response benchmarks~\cite{emobench2024, emobench2} show persistent interpretation bias.
This motivates explicitly separating linguistic behavior from affective ontology in our claims.

\subsection{Emotion, benchmark methodology, and robust prompting}
Related work across ToM, emotion, and social-signal evaluation consistently emphasizes: (i) benchmark family coverage, (ii) perturbation tests, and (iii) transparent statistical reporting.
Our pipeline is aligned with this design tradition through automated retrieval, multi-gate screening, scenario coverage diagnostics, and failure-aware orchestration.

\subsection{Research gap addressed by this study}
Most prior work either focuses on benchmark scores or user-perception effects.
Our contribution is narrower but operationally important: building a continuous, auditable, and reproducible chain for regret-like marker testing under controlled deprivation/counterfactual conditions.

\section{Research Design}
\subsection{Research questions}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Do deprivation/regret frames increase regret markers compared with neutral framing?
  \item Do persona instructions and temperature change marker strength and lexical variance?
  \item Can the full process remain reproducible under continuous automation?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Hypotheses}
\begin{itemize}
  \item H1: Deprivation framing raises counterfactual expressions.
  \item H2: Reflective personas increase self-attribution terms.
  \item H3: Higher decoding variance changes wording but does not erase prompt-condition effects.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Prompt and condition design}
Current bank: 436 scenarios, 311 personas, 5 temperatures (0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.7, 1.0).
Generation conditions are the Cartesian product of scenario group, persona group, and temperature.

\subsection{Markers and analysis}
For each sample we compute:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Counterfactual marker frequency.
  \item Regret-word frequency.
  \item Condition-aggregated means/standard deviations.
  \item Basic entropy/coverage checks.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Inferential analysis plan with real-model outputs}
For real-model outputs, we pre-register a three-factor design with factors: condition (deprivation framing), persona, and temperature.
We model marker rate $y$ as a mixed-effects regression with crossed prompt-item effects:

\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
    y_{ijkl} &= \beta_0 + \beta_1 C_i + \beta_2 P_j + \beta_3 T_k + \beta_4(C_i\times P_j) \\
    &\quad + \beta_5(C_i\times T_k) + u_l + \epsilon_{ijkl}
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $C$, $P$, and $T$ represent condition, persona, and temperature, $u_l$ is a random intercept for scenario prompt $l$, and $\epsilon_{ijkl}$ is residual error.
Primary confirmatory tests are Welch $t$-tests for paired contrasts and fixed-effects ANOVA equivalents with robust standard errors.
Effect-size reporting follows Cohen's $d$ for pairwise contrasts and partial $\eta^2$ for factors.

\subsection{Current evidence status}
As of this version, the full pipeline is implemented end-to-end, but the above inferential analyses are based on mock-data stress tests.
Thus statistical inferences are staged as pre-registered analysis code and will be finalized once real-model samples are collected.

\section{Discussion}
This study currently demonstrates a reproducible research stack.
The primary contribution is not final behavioral claims, but an auditable system that links literature screening, experimental design, and execution state.

\subsection{Limitations}
The current outputs are synthetic.
Hence interpretations are provisional and should not be used as final psychological conclusions.
