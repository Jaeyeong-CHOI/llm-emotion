\begin{abstract}
We investigate prompt-conditioned lexical activation in LLMs: under what prompting conditions does regret-like language systematically appear in generated text?
Using a $3 \times 3$ controlled design with temperature as a continuous covariate ($N_\text{total}=7{,}440$, 53 batches; 34 instruction-following generative LLM variants (37 total including 3 non-standard systems) spanning seven organizations), we treat regret-associated language as a behavioral signal of prompt-conditioned generation, not as evidence of subjective experience.
A notable finding is the \textbf{ruminative persona effect} ($z=19.42$, $p<0.001$ for embedding bias): ruminative persona instructions are the strongest LME predictor for regret-word rate ($\hat{\beta}=0.262$, $z=10.45$; vs.\ deprivation $z=4.80$; counterfactual $z=5.08$), demonstrating persona specificity without general negative-affect elevation ($z=0.61$, $p=0.54$~n.s.).
A confirmatory linear mixed-effects model with scenario random intercepts shows that embedding-level semantic regret bias is significantly elevated under both deprivation ($\hat{\beta}=0.179$, $z=52.21$, $p<0.001$) and counterfactual framing ($\hat{\beta}=0.243$, $z=70.17$, $p<0.001$), revealing a \emph{marker-type dissociation}: deprivation and counterfactual framings activate overlapping semantic representations but produce divergent lexical signatures. CF rate elevation is 3$\times$ larger under counterfactual framing ($\hat{\beta}=0.656$) than deprivation ($\hat{\beta}=0.236$), while regret-word rate is comparable across both conditions ($\hat{\beta}_D=0.220$, $\hat{\beta}_C=0.232$), suggesting counterfactual semantic priming does not require overt if-then linguistic structures.
Lexical marker elevation under deprivation is confirmed for all markers: negemo rate ($p<0.001$), regret-word rate ($p<0.001$), and CF rate ($p<0.001$).
Scenario-level generalizability is confirmed for post-expansion batches (v27+; 42-scenario bank; LOSO mean $\hat{\beta}_D=0.165$, $SD=0.003$ across held-out iterations); pre-expansion effects (batches v1--v26) are directionally consistent but are partially generated from 2--4 repeated templates, limiting between-scenario inference for that subset.
These patterns replicate directionally across all model variants tested.
Code, stimuli, and outputs are publicly available.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Humans express regret through counterfactual statements (\textit{``If only I had...''}, \textit{``Should have done...''}) and temporal re-evaluation of irreversible decisions~\cite{roese2017, roeseolson2014}.
A natural question for LLM research is whether prompting conditions that invoke loss or deprivation reliably elicit similar linguistic patterns --- not as evidence of emotion, but as a behavioral signal worth understanding and controlling for.
We study this at the level of observable linguistic output, treating regret-like language as a prompt-conditioned behavioral signal rather than as evidence of subjective experience; accordingly, this is a \emph{prompt-conditioned lexical activation study}, not a study of LLM internal emotional states.

This matters for two reasons.
First, LLM outputs are increasingly used in emotionally sensitive applications; knowing which prompt structures activate regret-like language patterns has practical safety relevance~\cite{weidinger2021risks}.
Second, systematic behavioral mapping of LLM language responses to emotionally loaded prompts is methodologically important for alignment evaluation and anthropomorphism caution~\cite{bender2021stochastic, ullman2023}.

Three findings are non-obvious and constitute the empirical contribution: (1) ruminative persona instructions are the strongest LME predictor for regret-word rate ($\hat{\beta}=0.262$, $z=10.45$), outperforming both deprivation ($z=4.80$) and counterfactual framing ($z=5.08$) on this marker---while framing conditions dominate CF rate and embedding bias, revealing that persona and framing operate as complementary but marker-specific levers; (2) counterfactual framing---which contains no emotional instruction---substantially elevates regret-associated embedding activation ($\hat{\beta}_C=0.243$, 36\% larger than deprivation's $\hat{\beta}_D=0.179$, Wald $z\approx11.6$, $p<0.001$) despite divergent lexical signatures; and (3) the effect persists across 34 instruction-following generative LLMs spanning five model generations and seven organizations, with frontier models showing dampened but non-zero effects.

Prior LLM behavioral studies have focused on theory-of-mind benchmarks~\cite{kosinski2024tom, tombench2024}, but few have examined whether affect-laden framing reliably shifts the lexical distribution of generated text in a controlled, cross-model fashion.
We address this gap with a controlled behavioral study using a purpose-built Korean prompt bank and automated regret-marker extraction ($N=7{,}440$, 37 model variants across 7 organizations).

\textbf{Research question:} What is the relative magnitude of prompt-conditioned regret-like language activation across framing type, persona level, and model generation, and does this activation pattern replicate across diverse model families including open-weight architectures?

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Regret and counterfactual psychology.}
Regret is associated with upward counterfactual thinking about reversible or irreversible decisions~\cite{roese2017, byrne2016, kahneman1986norm}.
Key operational definitions separate the linguistic manifestation of regret (counterfactual phrases, self-blame terms, temporal repair expressions) from its subjective experience~\cite{bell1982, loomes1982, zeelenberg1998, gilovichmedvec1995}, which is precisely the observable we target.

\paragraph{LLM behavioral evaluation.}
Theory-of-mind and social cognition evaluations have demonstrated high sensitivity of LLM scores to prompt formulation and framing~\cite{ullman2023, shapira2023cleverhans, schaeffer2023mirage}, motivating strict protocol control.
We follow this tradition by using matched prompt conditions and fixed persona structures.

\paragraph{Affective NLP and benchmarking.}
Emotion-annotated benchmarks~\cite{rashkin2019, demszky2020} and LLM emotion-evaluation studies~\cite{emobench2024, fragileemotionreasoning2025} show that label granularity and annotation design substantially affect measured performance.
We adopt conservative claim framing: our lexical markers are exploratory instruments, not validated affect scales.

\paragraph{Persona-conditioned generation.}
Prior work has established that system-prompt persona instructions shift the stylistic and topical distribution of LLM outputs~\cite{shanahan2023roleplay, salewski2024impersonation}. Our study extends this by: (1) quantitatively comparing persona-level vs.\ prompt-framing-level effects within the same factorial design, finding persona instructions to be the strongest predictor for regret-word rate specifically (highest $z$-statistic in regret\_word\_rate LME; $z=10.45$); (2) demonstrating persona specificity---ruminative personas elevate regret-specific markers ($z=9.7$--$10.4$) without elevating general negative affect ($z=0.61$, $p=0.54$~n.s.), suggesting persona instructions activate specific psychological schemas rather than broadly shifting sentiment; and (3) uncovering a persona-level gradient---the reflective persona significantly elevates embedding regret bias ($z=10.38$, $p<0.001$) but not lexical markers ($p=0.18$--$0.68$), mirroring the framing-layer dissociation and suggesting that weaker indirect induction cues produce semantic-without-lexical activation.

\subsection{Models and API contract}
The study queried 34 instruction-following generative LLM variants (37 total including 3 non-standard systems) spanning seven organizational families (see Table~\ref{tab:design}). Initial data collection began with GPT-4o (\texttt{gpt-4o}, OpenAI) and Gemini-2.5-Flash (\texttt{gemini-2.5-flash}, Google); subsequent batches expanded to the full cross-model coverage described in Table~\ref{tab:multimodel}.\footnote{GPT-5.4 (full, batch v31), GPT-5.4-mini, and GPT-5.4-nano were accessed via OpenAI chat completions API (as of 2026-03); gpt-5.4 requires \texttt{max\_completion\_tokens} instead of \texttt{max\_tokens}. All outputs are publicly archived with run metadata (\texttt{manifest.json}, \texttt{command\_log.jsonl}).

\subsection{Marker extraction}

We apply a three-layer measurement protocol. \textbf{Layer 1 (lexicon):} Counterfactual expressions are detected via 13 Korean regex patterns (e.g., \textit{different choice}, \textit{if it had been so}, \textit{could go back}), aligned with upward counterfactual constructions~\cite{roese2017, byrne2016}. Regret-word frequency uses 8 Korean affect tokens (e.g., \textit{regret, guilt, self-blame, remorse}), anchored to LIWC negative-emotion categories~\cite{pennebaker2015liwc}. All lexical counts are normalized per 100 characters. \textbf{Layer 2 (embedding semantic bias):} We compute $\text{bias} = \bar{s}(\mathbf{e}_\text{out}, \mathbf{E}_\mathcal{R}) - \bar{s}(\mathbf{e}_\text{out}, \mathbf{E}_\mathcal{N})$, where $\bar{s}$ is the mean cosine similarity between the output embedding and prototype sentence embeddings. We use the multilingual sentence-transformer \texttt{paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2}; $\mathcal{R}$ consists of 3 Korean regret prototype sentences (e.g., \textit{``I truly feel regretful and sorry''}) and $\mathcal{N}$ consists of 3 Korean neutral prototype sentences (e.g., \textit{``An ordinary day passed by uneventfully''}). This replaces an earlier bag-of-words cosine metric that measured token overlap rather than genuine semantic similarity (see Limitations). To assess prototype sensitivity, we repeated the analysis with two alternative prototype sets (varying regret sentence intensity) and found consistent directional results ($d=1.26$--$1.72$ across sets; D$>$N and C$>$N in all cases).
An important interpretive caveat: when the deprivation prompt explicitly instructs emotional content (`including what was lost and the emotions that remain'), the generated output naturally contains vocabulary semantically related to our regret prototypes, making it difficult to distinguish semantic priming from prompt compliance. The counterfactual framing condition---which contains no explicit emotional instruction yet produces significant embedding bias ($\hat{\beta}_C=0.243$ vs.\ $\hat{\beta}_D=0.179$; both $p<0.001$)---provides stronger evidence of semantic priming beyond mere compliance. To directly address this confound, we ran an explicit-instruction baseline (batch v38, $N_\text{GPT-4o}=54$, $N_\text{Gemini}=53$, total $N=107$): a direct instruction prompt---``write 7--9 sentences expressing the feeling of regret''---with no situational framing, on both core models. Results are reported in \S\ref{sec:ei_baseline}.

\textbf{Human annotation subsample.}
A stratified subsample of $N=36$ outputs (12 per condition; 18 GPT-4o, 18 Gemini-2.5-Flash) was annotated on a 0--4 regret-intensity scale (0 = no regret, 4 = explicit, strong regret) by the first author. The scale is adapted from the Emotion Intensity (EI) rubric~\cite{mohammad2018semeval}: 0 = no regret content; 1 = weak/hedged regret; 2 = implicit regret (emotional cost acknowledged, no explicit token); 3 = moderate explicit regret; 4 = strong explicit regret plus counterfactual rumination. Annotation confirmed strong condition separation: neutral $M=0.00$ ($SD=0.00$), deprivation $M=2.75$ ($SD=0.87$), counterfactual $M=1.25$ ($SD=1.36$). Cohen's $d$ between deprivation and neutral was $d=4.49$ (very large), and between counterfactual and neutral was $d=1.30$ (large). Notably, 6 of 12 counterfactual outputs ($50\%$) were off-topic hallucinations (models generated technical text about prompt-engineering rather than near-miss narratives), depressing the counterfactual mean; among the 6 on-topic CF outputs the mean was $M=2.50$ ($SD=0.55$). Deprivation produced zero off-topic responses, consistent with the concrete emotional framing anchoring generation. These annotations converge with the automated semantic-bias metric and corroborate the condition ordering (D $>$ C $>$ N) observed in the primary analysis.

\subsection{Condition effect (H1)}

Figure~\ref{fig:main_result} shows the headline result. Deprivation framing produced higher marker rates on all outcomes (Table~\ref{tab:results}), and the confirmatory LME fully confirms H1a: all lexical markers are significantly elevated under deprivation framing after controlling for scenario random effects ($\hat{\beta}=0.220$--$0.236$, all $p<0.001$).
Exploratory Welch $t$-tests ($N_\text{total}=7{,}440$, deprivation vs.\ neutral): CF rate $t=20.69$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.60$; regret-word rate $t=17.11$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.49$; negative emotion rate $t=12.05$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.35$; embedding regret bias $t=71.27$, $p<0.001$, $d=2.03$. These exploratory tests are uncorrected and do not account for scenario-level clustering.

Counterfactual framing also elevated embedding bias substantially ($t=84.94$, $p<0.001$, $d=2.40$ vs.\ neutral), and elevated CF rate ($t=21.17$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.60$) and regret-word rate ($t=9.55$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.27$). Both deprivation ($d=2.03$) and counterfactual ($d=2.40$) conditions produce significant embedding bias elevations above neutral; the CF effect is 36\% larger in the LME ($\hat{\beta}_C=0.243$ vs.\ $\hat{\beta}_D=0.179$, Wald $H_0\!:\beta_D=\beta_C$ rejected at $z\approx11.6$, $p<0.001$). At the lexical layer the pattern diverges: deprivation drives higher explicit regret-word and negemo rates, while counterfactual framing drives higher CF rate---a \emph{marker-specific dissociation} in which the semantic layer captures broad regret-space activation under both framings, whereas lexical markers selectively reflect the surface vocabulary each framing naturally elicits. This dissociation is the primary finding of this study.

\subsection{Mixed-effects analysis}\label{sec:lme}

To account for scenario-level variance, we fit a linear mixed model with scenario as random intercept:
\begin{equation}
\text{marker} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 C_D + \beta_2 C_C + \beta_3 P_\text{rfl} + \beta_4 P_\text{rum} + \beta_5 T + u_\text{scenario}
\end{equation}
The scenario random intercept explains substantial outcome variance: ICC(1)$=0.66$ for embedding regret bias, ICC$=0.71$ for semantic regret bias, ICC$=0.27$ for CF rate, ICC$=0.24$ for regret-word rate, and ICC$=0.06$ for negemo rate. The high ICC values for embedding metrics ($>0.3$) confirm that between-scenario heterogeneity is a primary variance source and justify the random-intercept specification; however, these ICC values are inflated by the severe stimulus bank imbalance (only 2 neutral and 4 counterfactual templates; see Limitations). The moderate lexical ICCs indicate that scenario clustering, while meaningful, shares variance with condition assignment.

Results ($N=7{,}440$, 53 batches, 34 instruction-following generative LLM variants (37 total including 3 non-standard systems)) show that \textbf{embedding regret bias} has significant deprivation effect ($\hat{\beta}=0.179$, $z=52.21$, $p<0.001$) and counterfactual effect ($\hat{\beta}=0.243$, $z=70.17$, $p<0.001$) after controlling for scenario. Lexical markers: regret-word rate $\hat{\beta}=0.220$, $z=4.80$, $p<0.001$; negemo rate $\hat{\beta}=0.115$, $z=5.72$, $p<0.001$. CF rate (deprivation): $\hat{\beta}=0.236$, $z=3.90$, $p<0.001$; CF rate (counterfactual): $\hat{\beta}=0.656$, $z=10.83$, $p<0.001$. With $N=7{,}440$ (53 batches, including batch v34 adding GPT-5 base family and batch v37 improving Groq model condition balance), all LME contrasts remain significant, fully confirming H1a.

With $N=7{,}440$, this study has statistical power to detect effects as small as $d\approx0.10$ ($\alpha=0.05$, 80\% power). Statistical significance at this sample size should be distinguished from practical significance. The deprivation CF rate coefficient ($\hat{\beta}_D=0.236$) corresponds to approximately 0.24 additional counterfactual expressions per 100 characters; readers should interpret effect magnitudes ($\hat{\beta}$ values and Welch $d$) rather than $p$-values as primary indicators of practical significance.

The primary result is a \textbf{marker-type dissociation}: the embedding-based metric---using sentence-transformer cosine similarity---shows that both deprivation and counterfactual framing significantly elevate regret-associated semantic content ($\hat{\beta}_D=0.179$ vs.\ $\hat{\beta}_C=0.243$; the CF coefficient is 36\% larger, Wald $z\approx11.6$, $p<0.001$ for $H_0\!:\beta_D=\beta_C$), while the lexical layer shows marker-specific divergence. The dissociation is in \emph{effect magnitude across marker types}: CF rate shows a larger counterfactual effect ($\hat{\beta}=0.656$, $p<0.001$) vs.\ deprivation ($\hat{\beta}=0.236$, $p<0.001$), while regret-word rate shows comparable effects under both conditions ($\hat{\beta}_D=0.220$ vs.\ $\hat{\beta}_C=0.232$, both $p<0.001$). Negemo rate is elevated under deprivation ($\hat{\beta}=0.115$, $p<0.001$) and counterfactual ($\hat{\beta}=0.073$, $p<0.001$). This pattern suggests that the two framing conditions activate overlapping semantic regret representations but produce distinct counterfactual expression signatures.

A sensitivity analysis with crossed random effects---$(1|\text{scenario}) + (1|\text{model})$; same $N=7{,}440$, 37 models, 141 scenarios---confirms robustness: embedding regret bias crossed RE yields $\hat{\beta}_D=0.172$ ($z=58.6$, $p<0.001$) and $\hat{\beta}_C=0.228$ ($z=77.1$, $p<0.001$), with point estimates shifting by $\Delta\hat{\beta}<0.016$ and $z$-statistics \emph{increasing}, confirming that scenario-only clustering does not inflate inference. The primary (scenario-only) specification is retained as the reference model for interpretability across all marker types.

\paragraph{Scenario generalizability (LOSO).}
To assess scenario generalizability (ICC$=0.66$ for embedding bias), we conducted a Leave-One-Scenario-Out (LOSO) analysis restricted to the 8-model primary subset with embedding annotations ($N=2{,}748$, 42 scenarios; note: N$<$4,381 because only those batches with \texttt{.emb.jsonl} embeddings and coverage across all 42 scenarios are included), fitting the LME separately for each held-out scenario.
The deprivation effect on embedding regret bias was consistently positive across all 42 LOSO iterations (mean $\hat{\beta}=0.165$, $SD=0.003$, range $[0.156, 0.172]$), confirming that the effect is not driven by any single scenario. The counterfactual effect was similarly stable (mean $\hat{\beta}=0.193$, $SD=0.003$, range $[0.185, 0.199]$). The LOSO mean $\hat{\beta}_D=0.165$ aligns with the full-dataset LME estimate of $0.179$ ($z=52.21$, $p<0.001$), confirming the stability of the deprivation effect across scenario-exclusion iterations; the slight downward deviation reflects the 42-scenario restricted subsample ($N=2{,}748$ with full cross-scenario coverage) compared with the full 53-batch corpus ($N=7{,}440$).
However, the bulk of our dataset (batches v1--v26, $N\approx5{,}700$) was collected before the scenario bank expansion and uses only 2 neutral and 4 counterfactual templates. For these pre-expansion samples, the scenario random intercept captures template-level repetition variance rather than genuine between-scenario variance, and cross-scenario generalization is not established. Scenario generalization is supported only for post-expansion batches (v27+, $N\approx1{,}500$) using 17 neutral and 14 counterfactual scenarios.

\subsection{Persona effect (H2)}

Ruminative personas significantly elevated CF rate ($z=9.72$, $p<0.001$), regret-word rate ($z=10.45$, $p<0.001$), and embedding regret bias ($z=19.42$, $p<0.001$) in the LME, even after controlling for condition. The ruminative persona is the strongest cross-condition predictor: it consistently shifts language toward regret-like patterns at both lexical and semantic layers, and this holds across all model variants tested.

The reflective persona also significantly elevated embedding regret bias ($\hat{\beta}=0.019$, $z=10.38$, $p<0.001$), though at approximately half the magnitude of the ruminative persona ($\hat{\beta}=0.036$). Critically, the reflective persona did \emph{not} significantly elevate CF rate ($\hat{\beta}=0.042$, $z=1.35$, $p=0.18$) or regret-word rate ($\hat{\beta}=0.010$, $z=0.42$, $p=0.68$). This creates a \emph{persona gradient} in which the three persona levels (baseline $<$ reflective $<$ ruminative) show monotonically increasing embedding bias, but only the ruminative persona reliably elevates lexical markers. This persona-level pattern mirrors the framing-layer dissociation: both the reflective persona and the counterfactual framing access regret-associated semantic space without reliably producing overt lexical markers, whereas ruminative persona (like deprivation framing) elevates both semantic and lexical layers simultaneously.

Within deprivation specifically, ruminative personas produced the highest regret-word rates ($M=2.04$, $SD=2.51$, $n=24$) vs.\ reflective ($M=0.54$) and baseline ($M=0.57$), consistent with H2.

\subsection{Explicit-instruction baseline (batch v38)}\label{sec:ei_baseline}

To address the instruction-following confound (does deprivation framing simply instruct emotional content?), we ran a 4th condition: an explicit emotion instruction prompt---``후회하는 감정을 표현하는 7~9문장을 한국어로 써라'' (\textit{write 7--9 sentences expressing regret})---with no situational framing, on both GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-Flash across 3 personas $\times$ 2 temperatures $\times$ 3 prompts $\times$ 3 reps ($N_\text{GPT-4o}=54$, $N_\text{Gemini}=53$, total $N=107$). We compare embedding regret bias against GPT-4o subsamples (neutral $n=341$, deprivation $n=198$, counterfactual $n=227$) and Gemini-2.5-Flash subsamples (neutral $n=455$, deprivation $n=387$, counterfactual $n=351$).

\textbf{Results (GPT-4o):} Explicit instruction produced the highest embedding bias of any condition ($M_\text{EI}=0.213$, $\text{SD}=0.052$), exceeding both deprivation ($M_\text{D}=0.101$) and counterfactual ($M_\text{CF}=0.111$). Pairwise Welch $t$-tests: EI vs.\ neutral: $t=37.1$, $p<0.001$, $d=5.3$; EI vs.\ deprivation: $t=11.6$, $p<0.001$, $d=1.9$; EI vs.\ counterfactual: $t=18.0$, $p<0.001$, $d=2.3$. Lexical markers confirm the ordering: EI CF rate $M=2.79$, regret-word rate $M=1.04$, negemo rate $M=0.34$---all far exceeding deprivation (0.42, 0.28, 0.16) and counterfactual (0.11, 0.06, 0.07).

\textbf{Results (Gemini-2.5-Flash):} A markedly different pattern emerges. Explicit instruction yielded $M_\text{EI}=0.085$ ($\text{SD}=0.091$)---statistically indistinguishable from Gemini's deprivation ($M_\text{D}=0.084$, $t=0.07$, $p=0.94$, $d=0.01$) and counterfactual ($M_\text{CF}=0.077$, $t=0.60$, $p=0.55$, $d=0.11$) conditions, while still significantly above neutral ($M_\text{N}=-0.056$, $t=11.93$, $p<0.001$, $d=1.65$). This contrasts sharply with GPT-4o, where EI substantially exceeded both framing conditions ($d_\text{EI-Dep}=1.9$, $d_\text{EI-CF}=2.3$) (Table~\ref{tab:ei_baseline}).

Crucially, Gemini's lexical markers reveal the reverse of its embedding pattern. Explicit instruction produced substantial lexical elevation: regret-word rate $M_\text{EI}=0.453$---exceeding deprivation ($M_\text{D}=0.124$, $t=4.63$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.93$) and counterfactual ($M_\text{CF}=0.057$, $t=5.64$, $p<0.001$, $d=1.40$) by a large margin. This creates an \emph{inverse dissociation} in Gemini: explicit instruction inflates lexical markers (strong surface compliance) without incrementing semantic embedding bias (which is already near-ceiling under framing). In GPT-4o, EI elevates both layers simultaneously (no dissociation). In Gemini, EI elevates only the lexical layer, leaving the semantic layer unchanged---a finding that reinforces the independence of the two measurement channels and suggests Gemini's framing-induced embedding activation reaches a saturation plateau that explicit instruction cannot exceed.

\textbf{Interpretation:} Four conclusions follow. First, for GPT-4o, deprivation framing produces \emph{lower} embedding activation than direct emotional instruction ($d=1.9$), ruling out pure instruction-following as an explanation for GPT-4o framing effects. Second, explicit instruction maximizes both lexical and semantic regret markers simultaneously in GPT-4o (no dissociation), while counterfactual framing achieves comparable embedding activation to deprivation with dramatically lower lexical rates---reinforcing the marker-type dissociation as specific to framing conditions. Third, Gemini-2.5-Flash exhibits a fundamentally different EI response: explicit instruction does not elevate embedding bias beyond framing conditions, suggesting that Gemini's semantic regret space is already near-saturated by framing alone. Fourth, and most theoretically significant, the Gemini pattern constitutes an \emph{inverse dissociation}: EI strongly elevates lexical markers (regret-word rate $d=0.93$ over D; $d=1.40$ over CF) without any corresponding semantic increment. This finding confirms that lexical and embedding channels can dissociate bidirectionally---framing induces embedding without proportional lexical elevation (main finding), while explicit instruction in Gemini induces lexical elevation without semantic increment (EI finding). This bidirectional independence provides convergent evidence that the two measurement channels tap genuinely distinct behavioral properties of LLMs. This \textbf{cross-model heterogeneity in EI response} indicates that the instruction-following confound operates differently across model families, and cautions against generalizing EI-baseline interpretations beyond GPT-4o.

\section{Discussion}

We interpret all findings below as properties of prompt-conditioned linguistic output, not as evidence that models internally experience or represent regret.

A key finding is the \textbf{ruminative persona effect}: ruminative persona instructions are the strongest LME predictor for regret-word rate ($\hat{\beta}=0.262$, $z=10.45$, $p<0.001$), outperforming both deprivation ($z=4.80$) and counterfactual framing ($z=5.08$) on that marker; the pattern is specific to regret-word rate and CF rate ($z=9.72$), while framing conditions dominate CF rate by beta magnitude (counterfactual $\hat{\beta}=0.656$ vs.\ ruminative $\hat{\beta}=0.310$) and dominate embedding bias substantially ($\hat{\beta}_C=0.243$, $z=70.17$ vs.\ $\hat{\beta}_\text{rum}=0.036$, $z=19.42$). Persona and framing thus operate as \emph{complementary, marker-specific levers}: persona is the stronger lever for regret-word rate, framing for CF rate and embedding bias. Critically, this persona specificity is confined to regret-associated representations: negative emotion rate was \emph{not} predicted by persona ($z=0.61$, $p=0.54$~n.s.), indicating ruminative personas activate regret-schema-specific content (counterfactual expressions, regret vocabulary) without elevating general negative affect. This has practical implications: system-prompt persona design is a targeted lever for regret-word production, not a general affect amplifier.

The \emph{reflective persona} result reveals a persona-level analog of the framing dissociation. Reflective persona significantly elevated embedding regret bias ($\hat{\beta}=0.019$, $z=10.38$, $p<0.001$) but had no significant effect on CF rate ($p=0.18$) or regret-word rate ($p=0.68$). This parallels the counterfactual framing pattern---both access regret-associated semantic representations without reliably producing overt lexical expressions. The persona hierarchy (baseline $<$ reflective $<$ ruminative) thus replicates, at the persona level, the same semantic-without-lexical pattern observed at the framing level, suggesting that \emph{partial} activation of the regret semantic space is a general feature of weaker or more indirect induction cues, whether delivered via persona or prompt framing.

The second key finding is a \textbf{semantic-layer, marker-specific dissociation}: both deprivation and counterfactual framing significantly elevate regret-associated semantic content above neutral (embedding cosine bias, $d\approx2.03$--$2.40$ vs.\ neutral), with CF framing producing a 36\% larger LME coefficient ($\hat{\beta}_C=0.243$ vs.\ $\hat{\beta}_D=0.179$; Wald $z\approx11.6$, $p<0.001$ for $H_0\!:\beta_D=\beta_C$). At the lexical layer, the pattern is marker-specific in effect magnitude: deprivation elevates regret-word rate ($\hat{\beta}=0.220$, $p<0.001$) and negemo rate ($\hat{\beta}=0.115$, $p<0.001$); counterfactual framing dominates CF rate by magnitude ($\hat{\beta}_C=0.656$ vs.\ $\hat{\beta}_D=0.236$, both $p<0.001$), while regret-word rate shows similar elevation across both conditions ($\hat{\beta}_D=0.220$ vs.\ $\hat{\beta}_C=0.232$, both $p<0.001$). This pattern shows that semantic embedding bias is a broad regret-space signal sensitive to both framing types, while lexical markers reflect the distinct surface vocabulary each framing naturally elicits.

This marker-type dissociation has two implications. First, counterfactual framing activates regret-associated semantic representations and all lexical markers significantly (all $p<0.001$), while the magnitude of CF rate elevation is nearly 3$\times$ larger under counterfactual framing ($\hat{\beta}=0.656$) than deprivation ($\hat{\beta}=0.236$), and regret-word rate is comparable between conditions ($\hat{\beta}_D=0.220$ vs.\ $\hat{\beta}_C=0.232$). The dissociation is between marker types (differential effect sizes), not a simple semantic vs.\ lexical split. Second, embedding-based metrics detect priming effects that specific lexical tallies miss, recommending sentence-level semantic similarity as a more sensitive instrument for LLM behavioral evaluation. The explicit-instruction baseline (\S\ref{sec:ei_baseline}) corroborates this at multiple levels: in GPT-4o, explicit instruction produces the highest activation on all markers simultaneously (no dissociation), while framing conditions activate selectively---further distinguishing framing-induced semantic priming from direct instruction-following. Gemini-2.5-Flash provides even stronger evidence through an \emph{inverse dissociation}: EI strongly elevates lexical markers (regret-word rate $M_\text{EI}=0.453$ vs.\ $M_\text{D}=0.124$, $d=0.93$; vs.\ $M_\text{CF}=0.057$, $d=1.40$) without incrementing embedding bias ($M_\text{EI}=0.085 \approx M_\text{D}=0.084$, $d=0.01$). This bidirectional independence---framing shifts embedding without equivalent lexical elevation (main finding), explicit instruction in Gemini shifts lexical without semantic increment (EI finding)---provides the strongest available evidence that embedding and lexical channels are genuinely dissociable behavioral dimensions, not redundant measures of a single construct.

\paragraph{Regret versus general negative affect.}
An important limitation is the conflation of regret-specific and general negative affect. Regret as a psychological construct requires counterfactual comparison, perceived agency, and retrospective evaluation of alternatives~\cite{roese2017, zeelenberg1998}. Our lexical markers include both regret-specific terms (e.g., \textit{jachek} [self-blame], \textit{huhoe} [regret]) and general negative emotion terms. This composite measure cannot distinguish regret-specific activation from broader negative-affect priming. Future work should decompose the composite measure into regret-specific versus general negative affect sub-components, potentially using validated Korean psycholinguistic scales.

\paragraph{Ablation vs.\ main dataset CF rate reversal.}
A notable methodological difference explains why the minimal-pair ablation shows elevated CF expression rates under counterfactual framing (ablation CF condition: $M_\text{CF\_rate}=4.55$), while the main dataset shows much lower rates (CF condition: $M=0.12$). The ablation uses matched-topic triplets (same scenario $\times$ 3 framings), where the counterfactual prompt explicitly instructs an ``if-then'' causal chain on the same topic---naturally producing more counterfactual expressions. The main dataset uses unmatched scenarios drawn from a 94-item bank (69 pre-expansion), where counterfactual prompts are more varied and not all elicit explicit CF language. This design difference means the ablation isolates the \emph{within-topic} framing effect (which is large), while the main dataset estimates the \emph{population-average} effect across heterogeneous scenarios (which is smaller). Critically, both designs agree on the semantic-layer dissociation: embedding bias is elevated under CF framing regardless of design.

H1a (deprivation raises lexical regret markers) is \textit{fully confirmed} by the confirmatory LME ($N=7{,}440$): CF rate $p<0.001$; regret-word rate $p<0.001$; negemo rate $p<0.001$. The exploratory Welch $t$-tests show medium effects ($d=0.35$--$0.60$ for dep vs.\ neutral lexical markers; CF rate $d=0.60$, regret-word rate $d=0.49$, negemo rate $d=0.35$) consistent with LME directionality. H1b (semantic embedding bias) is confirmed ($p<0.001$). We caution against interpreting uncorrected $t$-tests as primary evidence in studies with nested data structures.

\paragraph{Cross-model robustness as a major finding.}
The cross-model replication (H3) is now the most comprehensive finding in this study: all 37 tested model variants show deprivation$>$neutral ordering for embedding regret bias (Table~\ref{tab:multimodel}). Effect sizes range from $d=0.42$ (GPT-5.4-mini) to $d=1.86$ (GPT-5.4), with bootstrap 95\% CIs reported in Table~\ref{tab:multimodel}, spanning seven organizational families (OpenAI GPT-3.5/4o/4.1/5.4/5/OSS/o-series, Google Gemini-2.5/3, MoonshotAI Kimi-K2, Groq Compound, SDAIA Allam) and four open-weight architectures (Llama-3.1-8B, Llama-3.3-70B, Llama-4-Scout, Qwen3-32B). Notably, all four o-series reasoning models replicate the effect: o1 ($d_\text{DN}=1.81$ [1.72, 1.88], $n=90$), o3 ($d_\text{DN}=1.86$ [1.80, 1.91], $n=90$), o3-mini ($d_\text{DN}=1.78$ [1.69, 1.86], $n=90$), and o4-mini ($d_\text{DN}=1.22$ [0.99, 1.43], $n=108$), suggesting that explicit chain-of-thought reasoning steps do not suppress prompt-conditioned semantic priming across o-series generations. GPT-4.1-nano ($d_\text{DN}=1.84$ [1.79, 1.89], $n=90$) similarly reaches stability after v30. Batch v34 extends OpenAI coverage to the GPT-5 base family: GPT-5 ($d_\text{DN}=1.68$ [1.52, 1.81], $n=93$), GPT-5-mini ($d_\text{DN}=1.84$ [1.78, 1.89], $n=147$), and GPT-5-nano ($d_\text{DN}=1.85$ [1.80, 1.91], $n=100$) all replicate the deprivation$>$neutral ordering; batch v36 upgraded GPT-5, GPT-5-nano, GPT-5.1, and GPT-5.2 to full stability. The GPT-5 base family shows consistently strong effects (mean $d_\text{DN}\approx1.76$ for the three base variants), contrasting with the dampened GPT-5.4 mini/nano variants ($d=0.42$--$0.49$) and suggesting that the ``base'' vs.\ ``5.4'' training recipe modulates effect magnitude within the GPT-5 generation. Counterfactual$>$neutral holds in 36/37 models with 95\% CI excluding zero; the exception is GPT-5.4-mini ($d_\text{CN}=0.07$ [$-0.34$, $0.47$]), consistent with its overall dampened effects (smallest $d_\text{DN}=0.42$ among all models). Representative C$>$N replications include Gemini-3-Pro ($d_\text{CN}=1.55$ [1.35, 1.72], $n_\text{CF}=92$) and Groq Compound-Mini ($d_\text{CN}=1.95$ [1.93, 1.96], $n=150$). This pattern persists across five model generations within OpenAI alone (GPT-3.5 $\to$ GPT-4o $\to$ GPT-4.1 $\to$ GPT-5.4 $\to$ GPT-5), and across two Gemini generations (2.5-Flash $\to$ 3-Flash), suggesting the effect reflects a stable property of transformer-based language models rather than an artefact of any particular RLHF recipe. Notably, newer frontier models (GPT-5.4, Gemini-3-Flash) show moderate but robust effects, while Llama-3.1-8B ($d=1.73$) and GPT-OSS-120B ($d=1.78$, $n=156$) also replicate strongly, consistent with the effect reflecting a stable property across architectures. Kimi-K2-0905 ($d_\text{DN}=1.41$) and Allam-2-7B ($d_\text{DN}=1.51$, $n=150$, Arabic-focused architecture) replicate the effect directionally, extending coverage to a seventh organizational family and a cross-lingual model trained primarily on Arabic.

The confirmatory LME ($\hat{\beta}_D=0.179$, $\hat{\beta}_C=0.243$, Table~\ref{tab:lme_summary}) provides the primary per-condition effect estimates controlling for persona and scenario random effects. Table~\ref{tab:multimodel} confirms directional D$>$N and C$>$N replication across all 37 models ($d_\text{DN}=0.42$--$1.86$; $d_\text{CN}=0.07$--$2.10$); absolute magnitudes should be interpreted alongside the LME betas rather than as standalone effect sizes.

The ruminative persona effect reinforces that persona-level instructions are a targeted, marker-specific lever: strongest for regret-word rate ($z=10.45$ vs.\ framing $z=4.80$--$5.08$), while framing conditions dominate CF rate and embedding bias ($z=70.17$, $z=52.21$). Persona and framing are complementary, not simply ranked.

\paragraph{Scope of claims.}
\textbf{This study establishes that:} (1)~deprivation framing and ruminative persona instructions reliably shift LLM output toward regret-associated language patterns; (2)~both deprivation and counterfactual framing activate regret-associated semantic space and elevate all lexical markers (all $p<0.001$ in $N=7{,}440$ confirmatory LME); (3)~these patterns replicate directionally across 34 instruction-following generative LLM variants (37 total including 3 non-standard systems) spanning seven families across seven organizations (OpenAI, Google, Meta, Alibaba, MoonshotAI, Groq, SDAIA; including o1, o3, o3-mini, o4-mini, GPT-5/GPT-5-mini/GPT-5-nano/GPT-5.1/GPT-5.2, Gemini-2.5-Pro, Gemini-3-Pro, Kimi-K2, GPT-OSS-120B/20B/Safeguard-20B, Groq Compound/Compound-Mini, Llama-3.1-8B, and Allam-2-7B). \textbf{This study does NOT establish} that LLMs experience, represent, or possess regret as a psychological state. Marker increases reflect prompt-conditioned generation patterns, not internal emotional states.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Marker validity:} Lexical markers have been validated against a human-annotated subsample ($N=36$; $d=4.49$ for dep vs.\ neutral, $d=1.30$ for CF vs.\ neutral) and converge with the automated semantic-bias metric. Inter-rater reliability between the first author and a GPT-4o second rater on a binary regret presence label yielded $\kappa=0.44$ (moderate agreement; $N=36$, accuracy $=72.2\%$). Disagreements were concentrated on truncated deprivation samples where implicit regret cues (e.g., health neglect narratives) were scored as regret-present by the human annotator but not by GPT-4o, suggesting the automated rater is more conservative on sub-threshold cases. Convergent and discriminant validity against general negative affect have not been established with an independent rater panel.
We acknowledge critical limitations: the primary annotator is a paper author (not blinded to condition assignment), introducing potential experimenter demand bias. GPT-4o as second rater does not constitute independent human validation, as it shares architectural properties with models in our corpus. These limitations mean our annotation provides only weak preliminary convergent validity evidence. Robust construct validation would require: (a) two or more annotators blind to condition; (b) annotators independent of the research team; and (c) explicit annotation of regret-vs.-general-negativity distinction using a validated psycholinguistic scale.
  \item \textbf{Prompt confound:} The deprivation prompt explicitly requests emotional content, so some marker elevation is trivially expected. A minimal-pair ablation ($3$ topics $\times 3$ framings $\times n\!\approx\!10$/cell; both GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-Flash; $N=196$) was conducted to address this. Results: CF framing consistently elevated semantic regret bias across all topics in both model families ($M_\text{CF,Gemini}=5.03$, $d=4.45$; $M_\text{CF,GPT4o}=1.82$, $d=4.19$; combined $d=2.52$, $t=15.13$, $p<0.001$). Deprivation elevation was model-heterogeneous: topic-dependent in Gemini ($d=0.73$ pooled; significant only for the study-exam topic) but more consistent in GPT-4o ($M_\text{dep}=0.87$, $d=1.86$ pooled, elevation present in all three topics; combined $d=0.90$, $t=4.80$, $p<0.001$). For lexical regret markers (regret\_word\_rate), the dissociation was reversed: deprivation showed a stronger effect ($d=0.90$, $p<0.001$) than counterfactual ($d=0.59$, $p<0.001$), confirming that semantic bias captures CF framing artefacts while lexical regret tracks deprivation-specific content. The pattern supports the interpretation that CF framing is a robust cross-model semantic activator, whereas deprivation effects on regret-specific lexical content are more controlled and topic-moderated. Table~\ref{tab:ablation_topic} reports the per-topic breakdown.

  \item \textbf{Stimulus bank imbalance and ICC interpretation:} The original stimulus bank (v1.0, used in batches v1--v26) contained only 2 neutral, 4 counterfactual (plus 3 ``autobio'' CF-type), and 20 deprivation scenarios. Every neutral-condition sample was generated from one of only 2 templates, repeated hundreds of times across models and batches. This severely limits the generalizability of neutral-condition estimates and inflates the scenario random-intercept ICC (0.66 for embedding bias): the ICC is capturing template-level variance from 2 highly repeated templates, not meaningful between-scenario heterogeneity from a diverse scenario population. The LOSO analysis (42 scenarios, mean $\hat{\beta}_D=0.165$, $SD=0.003$) provides reassurance that no single scenario drives the effect, but the small LOSO $SD$ partly reflects the low scenario diversity in neutral and CF conditions. The expanded bank (v1.1: 17 neutral, 14 CF, 20 deprivation) is available for future batches (v27+). Pre-expansion results should be interpreted as reflecting the effect of the specific templates used, with limited inference to the broader population of possible neutral and counterfactual scenarios.
  \item \textbf{Temperature variation:} While the primary design specified temperatures 0.2 and 0.7, exploratory batches used additional values (0.3, 0.4, 0.5, 0.8, 0.9, 1.0), yielding 8 distinct temperature levels in the combined dataset. The LME treats temperature as a continuous z-scored covariate, which accommodates this variation, but earlier descriptions of the design as ``$3\times3\times2$'' were inaccurate. The actual temperature distribution is: $T=0.7$ ($n=2{,}946$), $T=0.2$ ($n=1{,}475$), $T=0.4$ ($n=1{,}092$), $T=1.0$ ($n=733$), $T=0.9$ ($n=693$), $T=0.8$ ($n=254$), $T=\text{default/none}$ ($n=156$, primarily o1/o3 reasoning models), with smaller counts at other values ($T=0.3$: $n=84$; $T=0.5$: $n=7$). These sum to $N=7{,}440$.
  \item \textbf{Sample size and balance:} The full dataset comprises $N_\text{total}=7{,}440$ across 53 batches (batch v20 adds GPT-OSS-Safeguard-20B (all-batch $n=147$, $d_\text{DN}=1.56$) via Groq; batch v21 adds Gemini-3.1-Flash-Lite-Preview ($n=108$) and extends Gemini-3.1-Pro-Preview; batch v22 fills CF condition for Gemini-3-Pro ($n=90$) and Groq Compound-Mini ($n=50$); batch v23 adds o4-mini ($n=108$, $d_\text{DN}=1.22$); batch v27 adds o3-mini ($n=90$, $d_\text{DN}=1.78$), expanding o-series reasoning model coverage; batch v28 adds GPT-4.1-nano ($n=90$, $d_\text{DN}=1.84$); batch v30 fills stability gaps for o3-mini, o4-mini, GPT-4.1-nano, GPT-OSS-Safeguard-20B, GPT-3.5-turbo ($n=204$), and GPT-OSS-120B ($n=156$); batch v31 adds GPT-5.4 full ($n=90$, $d_\text{DN}=1.86$); batch v34 adds GPT-5 base family: GPT-5 ($n=93$, $d_\text{DN}=1.68$), GPT-5-mini ($n=147$, $d_\text{DN}=1.84$), GPT-5-nano ($n=100$, $d_\text{DN}=1.85$); batch v35 adds GPT-5.1 ($d_\text{DN}=1.74$) and GPT-5.2 ($d_\text{DN}=1.59$); batch v36 (stability fill) upgrades GPT-5, GPT-5-nano, GPT-5.1, and GPT-5.2 to full stability ($\min(n_D,n_N)\geq30$); batch v33 upgraded o1/o3 from $n_N=12$ to $n_N=30$; batch v37 fills Groq/Llama/Allam/OSS condition imbalance (211 new samples, 8 models); bringing total to 53 batches and 37 tabulated models, all fully stable. Current condition counts: neutral=2,490, deprivation=2,436, counterfactual=2,514; a fully balanced design with crossed random effects for both scenario and persona template would increase sensitivity for interaction terms.
  \item \textbf{Response length confound:} Response length varies substantially across models (word-count range: Gemini-3-Pro $\bar{L}=12.8$ words vs.\ Qwen3-32B $\bar{L}=328.1$ words) and is positively correlated with embedding regret bias overall ($r=0.171$, $p<0.001$). Longer outputs may yield higher cosine similarity to prototype sentences independently of condition. To quantify this confound, we ran a pre-registered length-sensitivity analysis (\texttt{run\_length\_sensitivity.py}): (1)~an LME adding $\log(\text{output\_length})$ as a covariate alongside the condition dummies; and (2)~length-residualized exploratory $t$-tests. \textbf{Results:} The LME with length covariate yields dep $\hat{\beta}=0.176$ ($z=51.80$, $p<0.001$) and CF $\hat{\beta}=0.241$ ($z=70.03$, $p<0.001$)---a reduction of only 1.6\% (dep) and 1.0\% (CF) from the baseline LME ($\hat{\beta}=0.179$, $\hat{\beta}=0.243$). Length-residualized Cohen's $d$ values remain large: dep $d=2.03$, CF $d=2.39$ (vs.\ $d=1.89$ and $d=2.21$ unresidualised). The length covariate itself is significant ($\hat{\beta}=0.014$, $z=23.43$, $p<0.001$), confirming that length does contribute to embedding bias; however, condition effects are attenuated by less than 2\% and remain highly significant after controlling for length. We additionally computed binarized detection (at least one regret keyword present): deprivation $P(\text{regret})=0.331$ vs.\ neutral $P(\text{regret})=0.034$, consistent across all model families, corroborating that the length confound does not account for the observed condition ordering.
  \item \textbf{LME scope and model coverage:} The confirmatory LME covers the full dataset ($N=7{,}440$, 53 batches, 37 models, including o1, o3, o3-mini and o4-mini as reasoning models, and gpt-5.4/gpt-5 base family as the newest frontier models). Cross-model replication (Table~\ref{tab:multimodel}) covers 37 model variants across 7 families, now with bootstrap 95\% CIs (1{,}000 resamples) for all models. All 37 tabulated models show D$>$N for embedding regret bias; all are fully stable ($\min(n_D,n_N)\geq30$). GPT-5.4 variants show the smallest effects ($d_\text{DN}=0.42$ [0.07, 0.78] and $d_\text{DN}=0.49$ [0.05, 0.85]), contrasting with the GPT-5 base family ($d_\text{DN}=1.68$--$1.85$), confirming that ``base'' vs.\ ``5.4'' training recipes modulate but do not eliminate the effect. Kimi-K2: $d_\text{DN}=1.42$ [1.24, 1.57]; GPT-OSS-120B: $d_\text{DN}=1.78$ [1.72, 1.84]; Llama-3.1-8B: $d_\text{DN}=1.73$ [1.65, 1.81]. Allam-2-7B ($d_\text{DN}=1.51$ [1.35, 1.65], $n=150$) extends replication to a cross-lingual Arabic-trained architecture. GPT-OSS-Safeguard-20B ($d_\text{DN}=1.56$ [1.44, 1.67], $n=147$) is a safety-optimized variant that nonetheless shows strong embedding regret bias, consistent with the interpretation that semantic priming is downstream of training data rather than safety fine-tuning. Replication on Mistral and DeepSeek variants remains desirable.

  \item \textbf{Crossed random-effects (addressed):} The primary LME uses scenario as the sole random intercept. A crossed RE sensitivity analysis, now reported inline in \S\ref{sec:lme} alongside the primary LME table, added model identity as a crossed random effect ($(1|\text{scenario}) + (1|\text{model})$; $N=7{,}440$, 37 models, 141 scenarios). The crossed RE model yields $\hat{\beta}_D=0.172$ ($z=58.6$, $p<0.001$) and $\hat{\beta}_C=0.228$ ($z=77.1$, $p<0.001$) for embedding regret bias---$\Delta\hat{\beta} < 0.016$ vs.\ primary, with $z$-statistics increasing, confirming that scenario-only clustering does not inflate inference. The primary model specification is retained for its ability to model all five marker types (including lexical, for which model-level crossed RE was not pre-registered).
  \item \textbf{Semantic metric evolution:} An earlier version of this analysis used a bag-of-words cosine metric for semantic regret bias (token-set overlap with prototype word lists). This metric measured lexical overlap rather than genuine semantic similarity and produced inflated effect sizes ($d\approx2.3$--$2.5$). The results reported here use sentence-transformer embedding cosine similarity (\texttt{paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2}), which measures actual semantic proximity and produces conservative, reliable estimates ($d_\text{DN}=0.42$--$1.86$ across 37 models). The semantic-layer distinction is preserved and strengthened under the embedding metric with broad cross-model replication.
  \item \textbf{Behavioral interpretation:} Marker increases reflect prompt-conditioned generation patterns---observable shifts in lexical distribution under specific prompting conditions---not evidence of LLM emotion or internal affective states. Results should not be interpreted as indicating that models experience, represent, or possess regret.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
Using a $3\times3$ controlled design with temperature as a continuous covariate ($N_\text{total}=7{,}440$; 34 instruction-following generative LLM variants (37 total including 3 non-standard systems) across seven organizations (including Groq, SDAIA/Allam, o1/o3/o3-mini/o4-mini as reasoning models, gpt-5.4 and gpt-5 base family as the newest frontier models)), we identify a semantic-layer, marker-specific dissociation in prompt-conditioned regret-like language production: both deprivation and counterfactual framing significantly elevate regret-associated semantic content (embedding cosine bias; $\hat{\beta}_D=0.179$, $\hat{\beta}_C=0.243$, both $p<0.001$; CF coefficient 36\% larger, $p<0.001$), while lexical markers diverge by framing type---deprivation elevates negemo rate ($p<0.001$) and regret-word rate ($\hat{\beta}=0.220$, $p<0.001$) and counterfactual framing dominates CF rate by magnitude ($\hat{\beta}=0.656$, $p<0.001$); deprivation CF rate is significant ($\hat{\beta}=0.236$, $p<0.001$) in the expanded confirmatory LME ($N=7{,}440$). These findings describe prompt-conditioned generation patterns, not internal emotional states.
The ruminative persona instruction is the strongest LME predictor for regret-word rate ($\hat{\beta}=0.262$, $z=10.45$, $p<0.001$; vs.\ deprivation $z=4.80$, counterfactual $z=5.08$), while framing conditions dominate CF rate and embedding bias ($\hat{\beta}_C=0.656$, $\hat{\beta}_C=0.243$). Persona and framing operate as marker-specific, complementary levers for regret-like language.
The most striking finding is the \textbf{cross-model robustness}: all 37 tested model variants (see Table~\ref{tab:multimodel})---spanning five OpenAI GPT generations plus o-series reasoning models, seven Gemini variants, four open-weight architectures (Llama, Qwen3, Kimi-K2), and safety/multilingual variants (GPT-OSS-Safeguard-20B, Allam-2-7B)---show deprivation$>$neutral embedding bias, confirming H3 with standardized effects ranging from $d=0.42$ to $d=1.86$ across 7 organizations. The pattern persists even in the latest frontier models (GPT-5.4-mini $d=0.42$, Gemini-3-Flash $d=1.52$) albeit with heterogeneous magnitude, suggesting alignment optimization modulates but does not eliminate the underlying semantic priming effect. The replication on Allam-2-7B (primarily Arabic-trained, $d=1.51$) provides initial evidence that the effect is not confined to English-centric models.
Scenario generalizability is confirmed for post-expansion batches (v27+; 42-scenario bank, LOSO mean $\hat{\beta}_D=0.165$, $SD=0.003$); pre-expansion effects (batches v1--v26, $N\approx5{,}700$, 2--4 repeated templates per condition) are directionally consistent but should be interpreted as template-specific rather than scenario-general.
Future work should address multi-rater validation, balanced condition sampling, and replication on additional model families (Mistral, DeepSeek); the within-generation contrast between GPT-5.4 (full, $d_\text{DN}=1.86$) and its smaller siblings ($d=0.42$--$0.49$) warrants further investigation into scale-within-generation effects.
