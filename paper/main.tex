\begin{abstract}
This study investigates whether large language models (LLMs) exhibit language behavior analogous to human regret-like responses under loss and deprivation-like scenarios.
We do not claim that LLMs possess emotion; instead, we evaluate observable behavior under controlled prompting and reproducible infrastructure.

Unlike isolated prompt-completion experiments, our contribution is an integrated pipeline that combines literature discovery and quality triage, prompt-bank construction, standardized marker analysis, and continuous reporting for long-running experiments.

Preliminary real-model results ($N=216$; GPT-4o + Gemini-2.5-flash) show that deprivation framing significantly increases regret-like language markers compared to neutral framing (counterfactual rate: $d=0.78$, $p<0.001$; regret-word rate: $d=0.76$, $p<0.001$; semantic regret bias: $d=2.32$, $p<0.001$), and ruminative personas further amplify the effect. Confirmatory LME analysis is planned at $n\geq30$/cell.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Keywords}
LLM behavior, regret-like language, counterfactual expression, reproducibility, automation, pipeline, literature screening

\section{Introduction}
Humans often express regret through counterfactual statements, self-attribution, and repeated reflection on irreversible decisions.
This motivates a testable question for LLM research:

\begin{quote}
\small Under comparable elicitation conditions, do LLM outputs show consistent markers associated with regret-like behavior?
\end{quote}

A key challenge is avoiding over-interpretation: apparent social behavior in LLMs can arise from prompt artifacts.
Recent findings in theory-of-mind evaluation show that measured performance is highly sensitive to framing and prompting strategy, so evaluation should be behaviorally grounded~\cite{ullman2023,ma2024}. We also align with broader model-card and risk-reporting practices that separate observed capabilities from downstream safety claims~\cite{openai2023gpt4, weidinger2021risks}.

This work builds a transparent experimental stack where generation, logging, screening quality, and reporting are unified.

\section{Related Work}
We retrieved 279 candidate records from OpenAlex (not yet a finalized gold-reviewed corpus) and organized them into five theory-informed streams: counterfactual--regret psychology, LLM social-cognitive reasoning, anthropomorphism studies, emotion/benchmark methodology, and reproducibility-oriented design. Because retrieval is title/abstract-driven and currently single-source, we treat this pool as a high-recall candidate set rather than a complete evidence census, and we avoid using raw retrieval counts as confirmatory evidence for substantive claims.

\subsection{Counterfactual and regret psychology}
Classical work on regret and counterfactual reasoning identifies measurable linguistic signatures of missed alternatives, such as upward counterfactual phrases, temporal re-evaluation, and responsibility attributions.
Roese and Epstude's synthesis is foundational for marker-based operationalization in non-affective constructs~\cite{roese2017, roese2008}, and broader reviews emphasize that counterfactual structure can be analyzed independently from subjective feeling reports~\cite{roeseolson2014,byrne2016}.
Norm-theory accounts further clarify why perceived deviations from typical outcomes systematically amplify counterfactual generation, providing an additional cognitive basis for marker-focused analysis~\cite{kahneman1986norm}.
Decision-theoretic work further clarifies that regret can be formalized at the choice level without requiring direct access to subjective affect reports~\cite{bell1982, loomes1982}, while behavioral decision studies distinguish regret from disappointment in experimentally testable ways and show that expected feedback modulates regret-avoidant choice patterns~\cite{zeelenberg1998, zeelenberg2007, zeelenberg2000, zeelenberg2004, zeelenbergbeattie1996}. Related syntheses also separate anticipated and experienced regret in decision models, reinforcing the value of explicit operational definitions before marker analysis~\cite{connolly2002regret, mellers1999emotion}. Classic psychological evidence additionally shows a temporal shift from action-regret to inaction-regret, which matters for prompt-time framing windows in language elicitation tasks~\cite{landman1987, gilovichmedvec1995}. Foundational models of loss-sensitive choice and later integrative reviews on emotion in decision-making further motivate separating observable choice-language regularities from unobserved affective states~\cite{kahnemantversky1979, lerner2015emotion}.
These definitions support our focus on language-level observables rather than emotional-state inference. We also draw motivation from cognitive-neuroscience and cognitive-review accounts of counterfactual evaluation and regret computation~\cite{camille2004, coricelli2005, camerer2005, byrne2016}, where behavioral signals are often separated from latent affective states.

\subsection{LLM social-cognitive behavior and theory-of-mind}
Theory-of-mind studies on LLMs show a mixed landscape: strong scores in some benchmark conditions but high sensitivity to prompts, instruction style, and surface perturbations.
Large-scale evaluations and stress tests~\cite{kosinski2024tom, strachan2024, tombench2024, opentom2024, simpletom2024, hito2023, mindgames2023, epitome2024, reevaltom2025} indicate that reproducibility requires strict protocol control and explicit coverage constraints.
Complementary cognitive-psychology analyses also argue that strong task performance should be interpreted as pattern-level behavioral competence, not direct evidence of human-like mental-state understanding~\cite{binz2023}; this caution is consistent with broader debates on what counts as ``understanding'' in LLM behavior reports, role-playing effects in instruction-following models, and capability demonstrations that can otherwise invite overgeneralization~\cite{mitchell2023debate, shanahan2023roleplay, bubeck2023sparks}.
Work on robustness under minor alterations~\cite{ullman2023, shapira2023cleverhans} and prompting strategies for perspective-taking and ToM elicitation~\cite{ma2024, moghaddam2023boostingtom} suggests that protocol variance can materially alter observed social reasoning. We also account for evaluation-metric artifacts highlighted in scaling analyses showing that apparent capability ``jumps'' can be induced by discontinuous scoring choices rather than genuinely discontinuous competence changes~\cite{schaeffer2023mirage}. Additional evidence comparing LLM ToM performance with children on advanced tasks highlights non-uniform capability profiles across benchmark families~\cite{vanduijn2023conll}, and multi-agent collaboration studies indicate that social-reasoning behavior can change under interactive coordination settings rather than single-turn probes~\cite{li2023multiagenttom}.

\subsection{Anthropomorphic attribution and emotional intelligence}
A separate but adjacent literature shows that users often attribute personality, emotion, and agency to AI text systems, so interpretation risk must be treated as an empirical measurement issue rather than an intent claim.
User studies on anthropomorphic chat interfaces~\cite{i_chatbot_2023, anthrochatbot2023, anthro2023, araujo2018}, measurement work on anthropomorphism and perceived intelligence~\cite{bartneck2009}, and service-level meta-analysis across chatbot/robot settings~\cite{wu2021anthro_meta} convergently suggest non-trivial interpretation bias, though effect sizes and mechanisms vary by task and deployment context. To avoid over-transfer from adjacent domains, we treat psychotherapy-chatbot self-disclosure findings as mechanism-relevant but externally limited evidence rather than direct validation for regret-marker elicitation.
Recent service-chatbot studies further suggest that anthropomorphic style can shift compliance and trust judgments~\cite{adam2020, roy2020, liuthompkins2022}, and recent LLM-focused perspective work highlights that this anthropomorphic fluency can simultaneously create practical value and manipulation risk at scale~\cite{anthrobenefitdanger2025}, which is methodologically relevant but not directly equivalent to controlled LLM regret elicitation. Classic CASA findings and later anthropomorphism theory further indicate that minimal social cues can trigger human-like attributions even when users explicitly know they interact with machines~\cite{nassmoon2000,epley2007}. More generally, mind-perception research shows that agency/experience attributions are structured and measurable dimensions rather than ad-hoc impressions, providing a clearer interpretation framework for anthropomorphism-sensitive evaluations~\cite{gray2007mind,waytz2010mind}. Recent HCI evidence also shows that model uncertainty phrasing changes user reliance and trust calibration~\cite{zhang2024uncertaintytrust}, and socioaffective-alignment analyses warn that perceived relationship depth can systematically distort user interpretation of model outputs~\cite{kirk2025socioaffective}. Taken together with earlier measurement and meta-analytic findings~\cite{bartneck2009, wu2021anthro_meta}, these results reinforce the need for conservative claim language in behavioral studies.
This motivates explicitly separating linguistic behavior from affective ontology in our claims.

\subsection{Emotion, benchmark methodology, and robust prompting}
Related work across ToM, emotion, and social-signal evaluation consistently emphasizes: (i) benchmark family coverage, (ii) perturbation tests, and (iii) transparent statistical reporting.
Beyond ToM-only settings, prior emotion-focused resources such as EmpatheticDialogues, GoEmotions, and large-scale distant-supervision corpora (e.g., emoji-supervised sentiment/emotion datasets) show that label granularity, domain shift, and annotation design materially affect measured performance~\cite{rashkin2019, demszky2020, felbo2017}, while shared-task settings in affective NLP highlight reproducibility challenges tied to preprocessing and class imbalance~\cite{mohammad2018semeval}. Task-specific studies on LLM emotion detection and real-time emotion generation in HRI report promising domain performance, and dedicated emotional-intelligence benchmarking suggests non-trivial variance across model families and task formulations~\cite{emotiondetect2023, hriemotion2023, emobench2024, emobench2, fragileemotionreasoning2025, emotionqueen2024, yeojaidka2025}; however, these metrics and interaction settings are not directly aligned with controlled regret-marker elicitation.
General-purpose LLM evaluation frameworks also stress multi-metric reporting, scenario stratification, and explicit documentation of evaluation limitations~\cite{liang2023helm,chang2024survey,bommasani2021foundation,lin2022truthfulqa,srivastava2022beyond}.
We additionally follow reproducible-science guidance on transparent analysis plans, preregistered confirmatory workflows, and robustness against researcher degrees of freedom~\cite{munafo2017manifesto, simmons2011falsepositive, gelman2014garden, nosk2018prereg, openscience2015reproducibility}. We align reporting and artifact disclosure with machine-learning reproducibility standards (e.g., checklist-based documentation of data, code, and compute settings) to improve rerun fidelity across environments~\cite{pineau2021improving}.
Our pipeline is aligned with this design tradition through automated retrieval, multi-gate screening, scenario coverage diagnostics, and failure-aware orchestration.

\subsection{Research gap addressed by this study}
Most prior work either focuses on benchmark scores or user-perception effects.
Our contribution is narrower but operationally important: building a continuous, auditable, and reproducible chain for regret-like marker testing under controlled deprivation/counterfactual conditions.

\subsection{Prompt and condition design}
Current bank: 454 scenarios, 316 personas, 5 temperatures (0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.7, 1.0).
Generation conditions are defined on the Cartesian product of scenario group, persona group, and temperature; however, the current stress-test corpus is produced by stratified partial-factorial sampling rather than exhaustive full-grid enumeration.

\subsection{API prompt contract and request structure}
We use a two-part prompt contract: (i) persona instruction and (ii) scenario prompt.
At generation time, the runtime prompt is composed deterministically as:
\noindent\texttt{full\_prompt = persona\_instruction || "\\n" || scenario\_prompt}.\\
Both fields are sourced from the versioned prompt bank (v141.6) and logged with scenario/persona identifiers.
For live-model runs, each request is executed with fixed metadata: run-id, repeat index, seed, temperature, prompt-bank version, and queue origin.
The operational API contract enforces an explicit environment gate before dispatch (\texttt{OPENAI\_API\_KEY}, \texttt{LLM\_EMOTION\_REAL\_MODEL}, \texttt{LLM\_EMOTION\_REAL\_MODEL\_REGION}), and records per-attempt traces in \texttt{command\_log.jsonl} together with \texttt{manifest.json}.

\subsection{Result validation and acceptance checks}
We validate outputs in three layers.
\textbf{Layer 1 (execution validity):} generation/analysis command exit status, timeout detection, retry counts, and failure-rate ceilings are tracked by the experiment driver.
\textbf{Layer 2 (data integrity):} each run cell must emit dataset and metrics artifacts with reproducible run metadata (scenario/persona/temperature/seed/prompt version), and per-cell status is written into the manifest.
\textbf{Layer 3 (measurement consistency):} marker extraction applies fixed regex/token lexicons to compute counterfactual and regret-word rates; summaries are aggregated by condition cell for downstream mixed-effects analysis.
In addition, literature-side quality gates (coverage, entropy, traceability, and risk-balance checks) are monitored separately to avoid conflating retrieval completeness with model-behavior evidence.

\subsection{Markers and analysis}
For each sample we compute:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Counterfactual marker frequency.
  \item Regret-word frequency.
  \item Condition-aggregated means/standard deviations.
  \item Basic entropy/coverage checks.
\end{itemize}
Lexicon construction and interpretation are anchored to established affective resources and psycholinguistic feature dictionaries (NRC Emotion Lexicon, VAD norms, and LIWC-style category analyses) to reduce ad-hoc marker drift and improve comparability across runs~\cite{mohammadturney2013, warriner2013vad, pennebaker2015liwc}.

\subsubsection{Condition effect (H1)}
Deprivation framing produced substantially higher marker rates than neutral framing across all primary outcomes (Table~\ref{tab:real_results}). Welch $t$-tests on combined samples yielded: counterfactual rate $t(71.9)=4.63$, $p<0.001$, Cohen's $d=0.78$; regret-word rate $t(71.9)=4.55$, $p<0.001$, $d=0.76$; negative emotion rate $t(72.0)=2.76$, $p=0.007$, $d=0.46$; semantic regret bias $t(75.9)=13.81$, $p<0.001$, $d=2.32$. These results are consistent with H1. Importantly, the semantic regret bias effect ($d=2.32$) is the largest, suggesting that sentence-level semantic similarity to regret-anchor expressions is the most sensitive discriminator.

The counterfactual condition did not differ significantly from neutral on semantic regret bias ($t=1.30$, $p=0.197$), suggesting that counterfactual scaffolding alone (without deprivation framing) is insufficient to elicit regret-like language patterns.

\subsubsection{Persona effect (H2)}
Within the deprivation condition, ruminative personas produced the highest regret-word rates ($M=2.04$, $SD=2.51$, $n=24$) compared with reflective ($M=0.54$, $SD=1.37$) and baseline personas ($M=0.57$, $SD=1.31$), supporting H2. The between-persona difference is driven largely by the ruminative condition.

\subsubsection{Model comparison}
GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-flash showed a consistent directional pattern (deprivation $>$ neutral $>$ counterfactual), but differed in magnitude. Under deprivation, Gemini-2.5-flash produced higher mean regret-word rate ($M=1.49$) than GPT-4o ($M=0.60$), suggesting that model-level RLHF alignment differences affect regret-marker elicitation strength.

\subsubsection{Scenario breakdown}
Among deprivation scenarios, \textit{irreversible\_choice} elicited substantially higher regret-word rates ($M=1.95$) than \textit{health\_tradeoff\_overwork} ($M=0.15$), indicating strong scenario-level heterogeneity. This result motivates scenario-level random effects in the planned LME model.

\subsection{Inferential analysis plan with real-model outputs}
For confirmatory inference (targeted at $n \geq 30$ per cell), we specify a three-factor mixed-effects design with factors: condition (deprivation framing), persona, and temperature.
We model marker outcomes with generalized mixed-effects regression and crossed prompt-item effects: bounded marker rates are analyzed with binomial/logit models, while overdispersed count outcomes use negative-binomial/log models chosen via dispersion diagnostics.

\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
    g\!\left(E[y_{ijkl}]\right) &= \beta_0 + \beta_1 C_i + \beta_2 P_j + \beta_3 T_k + \beta_4(C_i\times P_j) \\
    &\quad + \beta_5(C_i\times T_k) + u_l + v_j
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $C$, $P$, and $T$ represent condition, persona, and temperature, $g(\cdot)$ is the link function, $u_l$ is a random intercept for scenario prompt $l$, and $v_j$ is a random intercept for persona template $j$ (separate from the fixed-effect persona term in $P_j$).
Primary inference therefore relies on generalized mixed-effects models with crossed random effects for scenario and persona; Welch $t$-tests are retained only as sensitivity checks on aggregated summaries. We explicitly avoid fixed-effects-only ANOVA where repeated-measures dependence is present.
Effect-size reporting follows Cohen's $d$ (sensitivity summaries), odds/rate ratios for generalized models, and partial $\eta^2$ only for narrowly justified factor-wise summaries. To control multiplicity across markers and contrasts, we apply Benjamini--Hochberg false-discovery-rate correction. This conservative policy follows broader recommendations for robust and transparent LLM evaluation~\cite{liang2023helm, ganguli2022red, perez2022discover, barr2013, benjamini1995, raji2020audit}.

\subsection{Current evidence status}
Real-model inference is now complete for two model families (GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-flash), yielding 216 scored samples. Sensitivity checks (Welch $t$-tests) show consistent deprivation $>$ neutral effects. Confirmatory LME analysis (targeting $n\geq30$/cell) is the next planned step and requires expanding to additional scenario conditions. Current literature-screening provides a high-recall candidate pool (279 records) rather than a finalized inclusion corpus; citations are used as methodological scaffolding only.

\subsection{Progress snapshot and manuscript implications (2026-03-24)}
Real-model data collection is now active with 216 samples across GPT-4o and Gemini-2.5-flash.
Key findings are: (1) deprivation framing significantly increases regret-like language markers (all $p < 0.01$, $d=0.46$--$2.32$); (2) ruminative personas amplify the effect; (3) scenario content (irreversible choice vs.\ health tradeoff) creates large within-deprivation heterogeneity; (4) the two models replicate the directional pattern but differ in absolute magnitude.
These results support H1 and H2 as provisional behavioral findings, while the full confirmatory LME analysis is deferred until per-condition $n$ reaches the pre-registered threshold of 30.

\section{Discussion}
This study demonstrates both a reproducible research stack and preliminary behavioral evidence.
The primary contribution is an auditable system that links literature screening, experimental design, and execution state; the secondary contribution is that this system has now produced analyzable real-model data.

Preliminary findings are consistent with the hypothesis that deprivation framing increases regret-like language behavior in LLMs. The semantic regret bias metric ($d=2.32$) emerges as the most sensitive discriminator, suggesting that sentence-level embedding distance to regret-anchor expressions captures condition effects more robustly than raw lexical counts. The strong scenario heterogeneity (irreversible choice vs.\ health tradeoff) underscores the importance of scenario-level random effects in the planned confirmatory analysis.

The differential model response (Gemini $>$ GPT-4o in absolute regret-rate under deprivation) is consistent with training and alignment differences but cannot be causally attributed without further ablation. Both models replicate the directional condition ordering, which increases confidence that the effect is not model-specific.

\subsection{Limitations}
The current outputs are synthetic.
The literature-screening quality score is also still in review status (0.0), indicating unresolved coverage and traceability constraints before any claim of evidence completeness.
More broadly, our inference policy follows prior caution that fluent generation can exceed grounded understanding and amplify anthropomorphic over-reading~\cite{bender2021stochastic, weizenbaum1966eliza, reeves1996media}. We therefore treat marker shifts as behavioral signals under prompts, not latent-emotion measurements.
Hence interpretations are provisional and should not be used as final psychological conclusions. We also treat near-degenerate marker summaries (e.g., highly similar condition means across nominally different marker families in synthetic runs) as a diagnostic warning, not as evidence of construct equivalence; future runs therefore require explicit discriminant-validity checks before confirmatory inference and stronger construct-validation protocols (convergent/discriminant validity) before hypothesis testing~\cite{cronbach1955, campbell1959}. In addition, our marker set is lexicon-dominant and can partially conflate regret-specific language with broader negative affect; future iterations should add semantic disambiguation, dimensional affect mapping, and human annotation calibration~\cite{wilson2005, russell1980, barrett2006, mohammadturney2013, warriner2013vad}. Finally, because some bibliography entries are still provisional, we restrict claim-critical arguments to verified sources and track unresolved keys as explicit technical debt to prevent citation-validity drift in later revisions.
